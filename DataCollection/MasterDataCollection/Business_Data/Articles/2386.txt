AdvertisementSupported byBy The Associated PressUnited States stocks struggled through a turbulent afternoon of trading on Tuesday and eked out small gains, led by utility and consumer stocks. The price of crude oil continued to fall, and energy and mining stocks tumbled.Stocks in Asia and Europe rallied earlier in the day as investors appeared to be satisfied with a report on China’s fourth-quarter economic growth. The Dow Jones industrial average rose as much as 183 points in the first minutes of trading on Tuesday. But the gains faded in the afternoon before a late spurt of buying in the last half-hour sent indexes mostly higher.The Dow rose 27.94 points, or 0.2 percent, to 16,016.02. The Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index rose one point to 1,881.33. The Nasdaq fell 11.47 points, or 0.3 percent, to 4,476.95. The Dow and S.&P. 500 are coming off their worst year-opening weeks in history. The Chinese government’s report confirmed that the world’s second-largest economy was slowing, as annual growth hit a 25-year low in 2015. Fears about a slowdown in China, and how abrupt and painful it might be, have helped knock oil prices to 12-year lows.Safe-play stocks like utilities and telecommunications companies rose the most. AT&T added 51 cents, or 1.5 percent, to $34.51. NextEra Energy gained $2.55, or 2.4 percent, to $107.81. The consumer goods maker Procter & Gamble, the maker of Tide detergent and Charmin toilet paper, gained $1.75, or 2.3 percent, to $76.73. United States crude fell 82 cents to close at $29.57 a barrel in New York. Brent crude, a benchmark for international oils, rose 21 cents to close at $28.76 a barrel in London. Energy stocks continued to fall on concerns about reduced worldwide demand. Chesapeake Energy lost 47 cents, or 13.2 percent, to $3.09. Marathon Oil fell 47 cents, or 5.7 percent, to $7.68. The price of gold fell $1.60 to $1,089.90 an ounce. Silver rose 22.5 cents, or 1.6 percent, to $14.121 an ounce. Copper gained 3.4 cents, or 1.7 percent, to $1.978 a pound. The gold miner Newmont Mining lost $1.38, or 7.8 percent, to $16.30, and the copper producer Freeport-McMoRan gave up 40 cents, or 9.2 percent, to $3.95. Freeport-McMoRan shares have skidded 41.5 percent in 2016.Foreign holdings of United States Treasury securities increased in November to the highest point since June, led by a gain from China, according to the Treasury Department. Total holdings of United States government bonds by foreigners rose 1.3 percent to $6.13 trillion after falling 1 percent in October. It was the highest level since foreign holdings hit $6.18 trillion in June. China, the largest foreign owner of United States government debt, increased its holdings by 0.8 percent to $1.26 trillion. The yield on the 10-year Treasury note, which has slumped this year, declined to 2.03 percent.Delta Air Lines reported a bigger fourth-quarter profit because of falling fuel prices. Delta said it expected fuel to be even less expensive in the first quarter. Its shares rose $1.44, or 3.3 percent, to $45.94. The health insurer UnitedHealth Group posted stronger-than-expected results in the fourth quarter. Its stock rose $3.26, or 3 percent, to $112.53. Shares of IBM were down 1.5 percent in extended trading after the company reported another drop in revenue on Tuesday, although its fourth-quarter earnings were slightly better than Wall Street expected.The jewelry retailer Tiffany fell after reporting that sales dropped in the fourth quarter and saying that it would eliminate some jobs. The company also forecast minimal earnings and sales growth in 2016. The stock lost $3.44, or 5.1 percent, to $64.21. The CAC 40 in France rose 2 percent and the German DAX added 1.5 percent. The FTSE 100 gained 1.7 percent in Britain. In, China, the Shanghai composite surged 3.2 percent and the Hang Seng in Hong Kong gained 2.1 percent. In Japan, the Nikkei 225 inched up 0.6 percent.The United States dollar rose to 117.56 yen from 116.95. The euro rose to $1.0908 from $1.0885. Bond prices slipped. The yield on the 10-year Treasury note, which has slumped this year, rose to 2.06 percent from 2.04 percent.In other trading of energy futures, the price of wholesale gasoline inched up 0.5 cents to $1.026 a gallon. Heating oil fell 2.6 cents to 90.9 cents a gallon. Natural gas slipped 0.9 cent to $2.091 per 1,000 cubic feet. Advertisement
