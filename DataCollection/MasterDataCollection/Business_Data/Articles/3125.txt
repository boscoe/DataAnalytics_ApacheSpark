By THE ASSOCIATED PRESSJAN. 25, 2016
Stocks fell on Monday as the price of oil slumped again, giving up some of the ground it gained late last week.The market opened lower and stayed in the red all day. Selling accelerated in the last hour of trading. The biggest losses came in the energy sector and companies that make chemicals and paper goods.The Dow Jones industrial average fell 208.29 points, or 1.3 percent, to 15,885.22. The Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index shed 29.82 points, or 1.6 percent, to 1,877.08. The Nasdaq composite index lost 72.69 points, or 1.6 percent, to 4,518.49.Plunging oil prices have been decimating profits at energy companies, and investors are worried that the global economy is slowing down. Companies that mine metals, especially copper, face the same problem.AdvertisementLow oil prices are also hurting banking stocks because some banks hold large amounts of loans from energy companies, and investors fear they may not get paid back.AdvertisementThe price of benchmark United States crude fell $1.85, or 5.7 percent, to $30.34 a barrel in New York. Brent crude, a benchmark for international oils, lost $1.68, or 5.2 percent, to $30.50 a barrel in London. United States oil jumped 9 percent on Friday after setting 12-year lows earlier in the week.

Exxon Mobil lost $2.59, or 3.4 percent, to $73.98 and Chevron fell $2.65, or 3.2 percent, to $80.89. Chesapeake Energy lost 56 cents, or 16 percent, to $2.95.Shares of Halliburton dropped 3 percent after the oil services company reported a loss in its fourth quarter and said its revenue had dropped 42 percent from a year ago. Halliburton has been hit hard as oil and gas operators cut spending because of falling oil prices and demand.Paper and packaging companies fell on concerns about falling product prices. WestRock gave up $5.63, or 14.9 percent, to $32.11 and International Paper declined $3.87, or 10.6 percent, to $32.58.Mark Wilde, managing director of BMO Capital Markets, said stocks in that sector were falling because an influential trade publication estimated that prices for containerboard fell sharply in January.“I think it confirms people’s fears,” Mr. Wilde said. “Falling prices are going to mean lower earnings.”Friday was the best day for the S.&P. 500 since early December; the Nasdaq had its biggest gain since September. Over all, it was the market’s first gain in the last four weeks.AdvertisementThe shaky global outlook helped push companies to make a lot of big deals last year, and that trend continued as Tyco International and Johnson Controls said they would merge. Tyco makes fire suppression systems and Johnson Controls makes ventilation systems, auto seating and car batteries. Both stocks have struggled as investors worried about their growth.

Tyco jumped $3.56, or 11.6 percent, to $34.15, the biggest gain in the S.&P. 500. Johnson Controls lost $1.39, or 3.9 percent, to $34.21.The heavy machinery maker Caterpillar sank after Goldman Sachs downgraded its stock to sell. A Goldman analyst, Jerry Revich, said companies worldwide were spending less money on machinery because commodity prices had dropped. Caterpillar lost $3.07, or 5 percent, to $57.91.McDonald’s rose after the restaurant chain said its United States sales grew 5.7 percent in the fourth quarter, its best result in more than three years. The company said its all-day breakfast menu and the warm weather had helped its sales. Over all, its sales rose 5 percent. In Japan, the Nikkei 225 rose 0.9 percent and the Hong Kong Hang Seng jumped 1.4 percent. Heating oil dropped 6 cents, or 6.1 percent, to 93.5 cents a gallon. Natural gas rose 1.9 cents, to $2.158 per 1,000 cubic feet.The price of gold rose $8.90, to $1,106.10 an ounce, and silver gained 19.7 cents, or 1.4 percent, to $14.254 an ounce. Copper fell less than half a cent to $1.998 per pound.United States government bond prices rose. The yield on the 10-year Treasury note fell to 2.01 percent from 2.06 percent. The euro rose to $1.0853 from $1.0795 Friday.
