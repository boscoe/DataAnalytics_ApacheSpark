By THE ASSOCIATED PRESSAPRIL 13, 2016
Stocks climbed again Wednesday as quarterly results from JPMorgan Chase gave banks a lift.Economic news from China powered industrial and technology companies in the United States and stock exchanges overseas.JPMorgan Chase, the largest bank in the United States, led a rally in financial stocks after its first-quarter results came in better than analysts expected. Railroad operators and auto parts suppliers also gained ground, while consumer goods makers struggled.Gains over the last two days have taken stocks to their highest levels of 2016.Julian Emanuel, equities and derivatives strategist for UBS, said it didn’t take much to send banks, the worst-performing sector in the market this year, higher.“Bank stocks have been so beaten up that any good news, either on better credit conditions driven by higher energy prices or news on cost-cutting, is likely to underpin those stocks,” he said.AdvertisementThe Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index rose 20.70 points, or 1 percent, to 2,082.42. The Nasdaq composite index advanced 75.33 points, or 1.6 percent, to 4,947.42.AdvertisementThe Dow Jones industrial average rose 187.03 points, or 1.1 percent, to 17,908.28.JPMorgan’s profit and revenue were bigger than analysts expected, and the stock rose $2.51, or 4.2 percent, to $61.79.Bank of America picked up 52 cents, or 3.9 percent, to $13.79, and Wells Fargo rose $1.26, or 2.6 percent, to $49.03. Citigroup increased by $2.35, or 5.6 percent, to $44.25.

Banks have slumped this year because investors are worried they will take big losses on loans to energy companies, which did in fact weigh on JPMorgan’s results. Low interest rates are also affecting bank stocks because they reduce the profits banks can make on loans.The railroad operator CSX gained $1.04, or 4.2 percent, to $26.03. The company’s profit fell as demand for coal got weaker and CSX hauled less freight, but expenses fell, partly because fuel costs dropped. CSX said it planned to cut more spending.Other railroad stocks surged. Union Pacific added $2.08, or 2.6 percent, to $81.72, and Norfolk Southern rose $2.42, or 3.1 percent, to $81.14.Industrial stocks and tech stocks rose on reports that China’s exports grew 11.5 percent in March compared with a year earlier. Tyson Foods dropped $2.74, or 4 percent, to $65.63, and Clorox declined $2.03, or 1.6 percent, to $126.14. Campbell Soup shed $1.17, or 1.8 percent, to $62.81.Delphi Automotive rose $4.07, or 5.6 percent, to $76.42, after the Internal Revenue Service dropped its contention that some of Delphi’s businesses were based in the United States and should be taxed accordingly.Verizon Communications slipped after around 39,000 landline and cable workers walked off the job Wednesday. Verizon’s contracts with its unions expired about eight months ago, and little progress has been made in negotiations. The stock declined 66 cents, or 1.3 percent, to $51.29.The CAC 40 in France rose 3.3 percent and in Germany, the DAX added 2.7 percent. The FTSE 100 in Britain rose 2 percent. Japan’s benchmark Nikkei 225 added 2.8 percent and in Hong Kong, the Hang Seng gained 3.2 percent.American crude slipped 41 cents, or 1 percent, to $41.76 a barrel in New York. Brent crude, the benchmark for international oil pricing, fell 51 cents, or 1.1 percent, to $44.18 a barrel in London.Wholesale gasoline changed little at $1.53 a gallon. Heating oil fell 1 cent, to $1.27 a gallon. Natural gas rose 3 cents, to $2.04 per 1,000 cubic feet.Bond prices rose. The yield on the 10-year Treasury note slipped to 1.76 percent from 1.77 percent. The dollar rose to 109.25 yen from 108.53 yen and the euro fell to $1.1285 from $1.1397.AdvertisementPrecious and industrial metals futures ended mixed.
