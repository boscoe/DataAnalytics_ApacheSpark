By THE ASSOCIATED PRESSJAN. 22, 2016
Stocks made their biggest gain in more than a month on Friday as oil prices surged again, lifting energy stocks. Tech stocks also climbed as Apple shares had their best day since August.Energy companies soared as the price of American benchmark crude jumped 9 percent.The Dow Jones industrial average rose 210.83 points, or 1.3 percent, to 16,093.51. The Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index had its best day since early December, gaining 37.91 points, or 2 percent, to 1,906.90. The Nasdaq composite index made its biggest gain since September, adding 119.12 points, or 2.7 percent, to 4,591.18.The markets rose this week for the first time in four weeks as the prospect of economic stimulus in Europe helped put investors in a buying mood after a dismal beginning to the new year. In Japan, the Nikkei 225 index had its best day in four months as investors hoped the Bank of Japan would also step in. Both factors strengthened the dollar.The gains were a hairpin turn from Wednesday’s action, when stocks nose-dived along with energy prices.AdvertisementWhile the two-day surge may have investors hoping the worst is behind them, James Paulsen, chief investment strategist for Wells Capital Management, said he still thinks the S.&P. 500 will slide to around 1,800 before a real recovery comes. “It’s going to continue to be a struggle,” he said. “Everyone will be convinced we’re heading for recession. Everyone will be convinced we’re in a bear market.”AdvertisementThere was little trace of that view on Friday. American crude rose $2.66 to $32.19 a barrel in New York. Brent crude, a benchmark for international oils, rose $2.93, or 10 percent, to $32.18 a barrel in London. The United States oil has climbed 21 percent over the last two days and has recovered about half its losses from earlier in the year.The pipeline operator Kinder Morgan rose $1.46, or 10.5 percent, to $15.34 after it jumped 16 percent Thursday. Williams, another pipeline company, added $3.70, or 23.1 percent, to $19.74, while Devon Energy gained $1.45, or 6 percent, to $25.63.Jeffrey Currie, a Goldman Sachs analyst, said energy prices had fallen so far that the industry was making real cuts in production. “We are now at a price level that is creating real fundamental change,” he said.Low energy prices are good for many industries and consumers, but investors have become anxious that they foretell a big slowdown in the global economy. Mr. Currie said it would take a long time for the market to recover, but he noted that energy prices were down because of a supply glut, not because demand was collapsing.

Shares of Apple, which have lost about a quarter of their value in the last six months, rose $5.12, or 5.3 percent, to $101.42. Microsoft gained $1.81, or 4 percent, to $52.29. Facebook added $3.78, or 4 percent, to $97.94 and Alphabet rose $18.79, or 2.6 percent, to $745.46.On Friday, the head of the European Central Bank, Mario Draghi, said the bank had a lot of options to raise inflation, and was determined and willing to act. On Thursday, he suggested the central bank would consider more stimulus at its meeting in March.The CAC 40 in France added 3.1 percent and the German DAX rose 2 percent. Earlier this week, the index entered a bear market, meaning it was down 20 percent from a recent peak. In South Korea, the Kospi gained 2.1 percent, and in Hong Kong, the Hang Seng added 2.9 percent. The Shanghai composite index in mainland China rose 1.3 percent.AdvertisementTelecommunications and utilities stocks also rose and turned positive for the year. Both were up 1 percent while the other eight industrial sectors in the S.&P. 500 are still much lower in 2016. Last year, the S.&P. 500 utility index fell 8 percent and telecom stocks fell 2 percent.Mr. Paulsen of Wells Capital Management said investors turned to utilities and telecom stocks when the market became volatile. Companies in those industries pay relatively large dividends, which means their prices are more stable and the stocks behave almost like bonds. “They are just the most conservative sector of the stock market,” he said.The credit card company American Express continued to experience fallout from its negative outlook for 2016 and 2017 issued late Thursday. The company expects its earnings per share to fall this year even though it’s selling credit-card accounts tied a co-branded credit card it offers with Costco. That relationship is ending. Its stock fell $7.58, or 12.1 percent, to $55.06, its biggest loss in almost seven years.The gold and copper producer Freeport-McMoRan tumbled 39 cents, or 9.1 percent, to $3.94. Its shares have dropped 42 percent in 2016 after huge plunges the previous two years. The company has struggled as metals prices have fallen, and its decision a few years ago to invest in oil and gas came shortly before those prices also buckled. Schlumberger, the largest oil-field services company in the world, said on Thursday that it cut 10,000 jobs in the fourth quarter after eliminating some 20,000 earlier in 2015. But the company simultaneously said it would buy back $10 billion of its stock. The shares rose $3.7, or 6.1 percent, to $65.20 on Friday.In other energy trading, wholesale gas added 5.3 cents, or 5.1 percent, to $1.084 a gallon. Heating oil picked up 9.8 cents, or 10.9 percent, to 99.6 cents a gallon. Natural gas inched up to $2.139 per 1,000 cubic feet.United States government bond prices fell. The yield on the 10-year Treasury note rose to 2.06 percent from 2.03 percent a day earlier. The price of gold fell $1.90 to $1,096.30 an ounce and silver fell 3.7 cents to $14.06 an ounce. Copper rose 0.6 cents to $2.003 a pound.The dollar rose to 118.78 yen from 117.50 on Thursday. The euro weakened on the prospect of further stimulus from the European Central Bank.
