AdvertisementSupported byBy The Associated PressThe stock market mounted a last-minute comeback to close slightly higher on Monday, snapping a three-day losing streak.The Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index and the Dow Jones industrial average each eked out a tiny gain, while the Nasdaq composite ended slightly lower.Consumer staples stocks were among the biggest gainers. Oil and gas companies were hit by another plunge in the price of crude, which tumbled 5.3 percent to a 12-year low. Chevron lost 1.7 percent and Exxon Mobil fell 1 percent.Investors have been increasingly uneasy about the trajectory of China’s economy and the possible implications for American companies’ earnings. China’s Shanghai composite fell 5.3 percent on Monday.“Investors have one eye on China, and all that’s going on there, and the other eye on oil,” said Erik Davidson, chief investment officer at Wells Fargo Private Bank. “Those two things are keeping investors on pins and needles right now.”The Dow added 52.12 points, or 0.3 percent, to close at 16,398.57. The S.&P. rose 1.64 points, or 0.1 percent, to 1,923.67. The Nasdaq fell 5.64 points, or 0.1 percent, to 4,637.99. All the major stock indexes are down sharply for the year.The three indexes hinted at a rebound early Monday, but spent much of the day in the red as investors weighed the implications of another stock market drop in China and the slide in crude oil. The market appeared headed for a lower close until the final minutes of regular trading, when the Dow and the S.&P. 500 shifted back into positive territory.The market action is a slight reprieve from an otherwise rough year so far for investors. Last week, United States stocks posted their worst week in more than four years. It was also the market’s worst-ever opening week of a year.A weakening of China’s currency and steep drops in its stock market have stoked worries over the outlook for China, the world’s second-largest economy. That does not bode well for the next round of company earnings, which begins this week.Many companies’ quarterly results will probably reflect the impact of China’s softening economy and lower oil prices, said Jason Pride, director of investment strategy at Glenmede.“The No. 1 most-mentioned item in third-quarter reports was weakness in China,” Mr. Pride said. “We’d be surprised if China and oil are not central to the earnings narrative as well.”Alcoa delivered its results after regular trading closed Monday. The earnings were better than analysts had expected. Several banks, including Citigroup, Wells Fargo and JPMorgan Chase, are scheduled to report results toward the end of the week.On Monday, benchmark United States crude oil dropped $1.75, or 5.3 percent, to $31.41 a barrel in New York. The last time it was lower was Dec. 5, 2003, when it closed at $30.73 a barrel. Brent crude, a benchmark for international oil, fell $2, or 6 percent, to $31.55 a barrel in London.Several energy and mining companies slumped as prices for crude oil and other commodities fell. Freeport-McMoRan shares sank $1.10, or 20.3 percent, to $4.31, making the company the biggest decliner in the S.&P. 500. Stock in Consol Energy also slumped, losing 69 cents, or 9 percent, to close at $7. NRG Energy shed $1.11, or 9.8 percent, closing at $10.23. Chevron shares slid $1.36, or 1.7 percent, to $80.77, while Exxon Mobil stock lost $1, or 1.3 percent, to close at $73.69.All told, energy stocks fell 2.1 percent, the most among S.&P. 500 companies. The sector is down 8.8 percent this year. That’s on top of a 24 percent loss for 2015.Consumer staples stocks led the risers, adding about 1 percent. Macy’s notched the biggest gain, adding $2.93, or 8.2 percent, to $38.82. The hospital operator HCA Holdings also rose after it raised its profit forecast. The stock added $3.56, or 5.5 percent, to close at $67.83.European markets were down. Germany’s DAX slipped 0.3 percent, while the CAC 40 in France lost 0.5 percent. The FTSE 100 index of leading British shares slid 0.7 percent.In Asia, Chinese stocks sank again after a rebound on Friday that analysts suggested was a result of buying from a group of state entities known as the National Team. The Shanghai Composite Index fell 5.3 percent and Hong Kong’s Hang Seng sank 2.8 percent. Sydney’s S&P/ASX 200 lost 1.2 percent, while Seoul’s Kospi fell 1.2 percent. Tokyo’s markets were closed for a holiday.In metals trading, gold fell $1.30 to $1,096.50 an ounce, while silver fell 5 cents to $13.86 an ounce. Copper slipped 5 cents, or 2.4 percent, to $1.97 a pound.Bond prices fell. The yield on the 10-year Treasury note rose to 2.18 percent from 2.12 percent late Friday. In currency action, the euro fell to $1.0849 from $1.0918 and the dollar edged down to 117.73 yen compared with 117.50 yen on Friday.Advertisement
