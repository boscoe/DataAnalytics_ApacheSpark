By THE ASSOCIATED PRESSSEPT. 27, 2016
Stocks on Wall Street rebounded and climbed on Tuesday after a survey showed consumer confidence at a nine-year high, a sign Americans would keep spending in the months to come. Technology and consumer shares made the largest gains.The market opened lower after two days of losses but quickly recovered. The consumer confidence report lifted major indexes, and they had locked in a big gain by early afternoon.The Dow Jones industrial average ended the day up 133.47 points, or 0.7 percent, at 18,228.30. The Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index picked up 13.83 points, or 0.6 percent, to close at 2,159.93. The Nasdaq composite index gained 48.22 points, or 0.9 percent, to 5,305.71.

Technology companies jumped, and solid results from the cruise ship operator Carnival sent travel-related companies higher. Energy companies slumped along with oil prices as hopes for an international cut in fuel production faded.AdvertisementUnited States consumer confidence reached its highest level this month since August 2007, according to a survey by the Conference Board. The group said its Consumer Confidence Index rose to 104.1 as consumers grew more optimistic about the labor market. The result was better than expected.AdvertisementKatie Nixon, chief investment officer for Northern Trust, said consumer confidence was even more important now because government spending, exports and capital spending by businesses were all limited.“Everything’s riding on the consumer right now,” she said.Travel companies made some of the biggest gains on Tuesday after Carnival reported a solid profit and strong revenue in the third quarter and raised the forecast for its full-year results. Carnival rose $1.88, or 4 percent, to $48.35, and its competitor Royal Caribbean Cruises added $3.38, or 4.8 percent, to $74.35. Southwest Airlines climbed $1.67, or 4.5 percent, to $38.54, and the booking site Expedia gained $4.01, or 3.7 percent, to $113.10.

Oil prices fell after investors were disappointed again that members of the Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries did not make a deal to limit production. United States benchmark crude fell $1.26, or 2.7 percent, to $44.67 a barrel in New York. The international standard, Brent crude, dropped $1.38, or 2.9 percent, to $45.97 a barrel in London. Devon Energy shares slipped $1.26, or 3.2 percent, to $38.38, and ConocoPhillips sank 59 cents, or 1.5 percent, to $39.43.American Express gained 86 cents, or 1.4 percent, to $64.28. A federal appeals court ruled late Monday that the company did not violate antitrust laws by preventing stores from asking customers to use one credit card over another and steering consumers to another type of payment. On Tuesday, the company said it would buy back as many as 150 million shares of its stock and raised its dividend to 32 cents from 29 cents.Bond prices continued to rise. The price of the 10-year Treasury note rose 7/32, to 9915/32. The yield on 10-year Treasuries fell to 1.56 percent from 1.58 percent.The Geo Group and Corrections Corporation of America slumped after the Democratic presidential candidate Hillary Clinton said during the debate Monday night that states should stop using private prisons. In August the federal government said the Justice Department would stop doing business with those prison companies. CCA fell $1.18, or 7.4 percent, to $14.78, and Geo slid 94 cents, or 3.8 percent, to $23.68.The price of gold fell $13.80, or 1 percent, to $1,325.90 an ounce.The CAC 40 in France lost 0.2 percent, and the German DAX fell 0.3 percent. The FTSE 100 in London was 0.2 percent lower. The Hang Seng Index in Hong Kong rose 1.1 percent, and the Japanese Nikkei 225 gained 0.8 percent.
