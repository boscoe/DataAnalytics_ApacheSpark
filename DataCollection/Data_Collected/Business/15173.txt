AdvertisementSupported byBy The Associated PressStocks on Wall Street took their biggest loss in almost a month on Tuesday as investors worried about the health of the United States economy and sold shares in retailers and car companies. Machinery companies also fell, and the price of oil declined again.Travel companies fell after the cruise line operator Royal Caribbean cut its projections for the year, and automakers and suppliers fell after car companies reported lower American sales for July. Banks finished lower as investors worried about the health of banks in Europe.While stock indexes set record highs as recently as July 22, shares have traded in a very narrow range for the last few weeks. Because stocks are not making big gains, investors are sensitive to signs of trouble for the economy, including weaker sales of autos or last week’s disappointing report on economic growth.“The market had just rallied,” said Jim Paulsen, the chief investment strategist for Wells Capital Management, adding that it had now “sort of paused.”“Any weak reports get magnified,” he said.The Dow Jones industrial average fell 90.74 points, or 0.5 percent, to 18,313.77. The Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index lost 13.81 points, or 0.6 percent, to 2,157.03. The Nasdaq composite index slid 46.46 points, or 0.9 percent, to 5,137.73. The Dow has fallen seven days in a row.Auto companies reported lower sales for July in the United States as a heat wave kept buyers at home. General Motors said its sales fell 2 percent and Ford said sales fell 3 percent. After six straight years of growth, auto sales have reached record levels and are starting to plateau. G.M. stock shed $1.37, or 4.4 percent, to $29.93. Ford lost 54 cents, or 4.3 percent, to $11.94. The car retailers CarMax and AutoNation and the supplier BorgWarner all fell.Royal Caribbean cut its forecasts for the year as the strong dollar continues to hurt its results. That left the cruise line operator’s stock down $4.51, or 6.3 percent, to $67.35. Other consumer companies like the retailers Nordstrom and Kohl’s and the office supply chain Staples stumbled.Industrial and transport companies struggled. Delta Air Lines said a revenue measurement fell in July and its stock lost $3.09, or 7.8 percent, to $36.39. American Airlines lost $2.09, or 5.9 percent, to $33.51. Emerson Electric, which makes valves and process controls systems, posted disappointing quarterly results. It gave up $2.75, or 4.9 percent, to $53.03.Oil prices continued to drop, extending a slide that has lasted more than two weeks. Benchmark crude fell 55 cents, or 1.4 percent, to $39.51 in New York — the first time it has closed under $40 a barrel since April 8. Brent crude, which is used to price international oils, sank 34 cents, to $41.80 a barrel in London.The August contract price of gold rose $13, or 1 percent, to $1,364.40 an ounce. Silver jumped 20 cents, or 1 percent, to $20.70 an ounce. Copper added 1 cent, to $2.21 a pound.The German DAX and French CAC 40 both lost 1.8 percent. In Britain, the FTSE 100 fell 0.7 percent. Japan’s Nikkei 225 index lost 1.5 percent. While the Japanese government approved a stimulus package worth about $274 billion, investors were skeptical it would work. The South Korean Kospi lost 0.5 percent to 2,019.70. China’s Shanghai composite index edged up 0.6 percent.Bond prices fell. The yield on the 10-year Treasury note edged up to 1.55 percent from 1.52 percent. The dollar fell to 100.85 yen from 102.36 yen, and the euro rose to $1.1219 from $1.1165.Advertisement
