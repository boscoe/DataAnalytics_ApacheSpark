AdvertisementSupported byBy Chad BrayLONDON — The British government said on Monday that it planned to sell a 2 billion pound, or $3 billion, stake in the Lloyds Banking Group to retail investors in the spring, as it prepares to exit ownership of the lender.In the midst of the financial crisis in 2008, Lloyds received a £17 billion bailout from the British government, and its British banking rival Royal Bank of Scotland received £45 billion.The British government had originally planned a retail offering in Lloyds shares last year, but it delayed those plans because of turmoil in the markets.“It is the government’s intention to fully exit from its Lloyds shareholding in the coming months,” the Treasury said in a news release, “and as part of this at least £2 billion of shares will be sold to retail investors.”The British government owned more than 40 percent of Lloyds after the bailout, but it has reduced its holdings since September 2013, as the bank’s prospects have improved.Last month, the government reduced its stake in Lloyds to just below 12 percent, as part of a prearranged trading plan that is being managed by Morgan Stanley.In August, the government began its first sale of its holdings in R.B.S., raising about £2.1 billion.On Monday, the Treasury said that members of the public would be able to buy Lloyds shares at a 5 percent discount to the market price and would receive a bonus share for every 10 shares they hold for more than a year as part of the retail sale. The bonus share incentive will be capped at £200 per investor.The government said it would give priority to the orders of investors seeking to buy less than £1,000.All proceeds from the share sales are used to pay down the national debt, the Treasury said.Advertisement
