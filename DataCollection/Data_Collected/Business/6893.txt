AdvertisementSupported byBy The Associated PressStocks edged higher on Wednesday after the Federal Reserve reassured investors that it was in no rush to raise interest rates from historically low levels.The central bank said that the economy was strengthening, but not enough for policy makers to signal an imminent increase in rates. The Fed’s benchmark rate has remained near zero for more than six years in an effort to bolster the economy and encourage borrowing, lending and investment.Surging stocks and low interest rates have gone hand-in-hand over the past six years, pushing the market to highs. Investors worry that, should the Fed raise rates too quickly, it could stifle growth.“This makes the market feel more confident,” said Alan Rechtschaffen, a financial adviser at UBS Wealth Management Americas. “What the Fed is telling you is that they’re in no rush to raise interest rates right now.”The Dow Jones industrial average gained 31.26 points, or 0.2 percent, to 17,935.74. The Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index rose 4.15 points, or 0.2 percent, to 2,100.44. The Nasdaq rose 9.33 points, or 0.2 percent, to 5,064.88.Bond prices rose after the Fed released its statement, reversing an earlier sell-off. The yield on the 10-year Treasury note fell to 2.32 percent from 2.38 percent before the statement was released. Eight of the 10 sectors in the S.&P. 500 index ended the day higher, with utilities leading the gains. The sector rose 0.9 percent but is still down 9.7 percent this year. The major stock indexes remain close to highs set in May but have sagged in the past month. The Dow is up 0.6 percent this year despite dropping 2 percent from its last record close on May 19. The S.&P. is up 2 percent for the year and off 1.4 percent from its high on May 21. The Nasdaq is faring the best. It is up 6.9 percent this year and off less than 1 percent from its most recent high on May 27.Aside from the Fed, investors were also keeping an eye on negotiation between Greece and its lenders. Greece remained deadlocked in talks with its creditors on Wednesday, and there was little sign of a breakthrough a day ahead of a meeting of the 19 finance ministers from countries that use the euro. Greece needs to get more loans before the end of the month, when its bailout program expires and it is scheduled to make a big payment to the International Monetary Fund.European markets have slumped in the last month as the talks have failed to produce an agreement. Greek markets are suffering the most. Greece’s benchmark index sank 3 percent on Wednesday and was down 18 percent this year.Yields on Greek government bonds have surged as investors’ confidence in the country’s ability to pay its debt has waned. The yield on the Greek 10-year government bond has climbed to 13 percent from 6 percent a year ago.In the United States, investors welcomed the latest round of corporate deals.Shares in Kythera Biopharmaceuticals surged 22.1 percent on news that Allergan, the maker of Botox, has agreed to buy Kythera, a California drug maker, for about $2.1 billion. The deal would add an injection that reduces “double chin” to the portfolio of products of Allergan, which is based in Dublin. Kythera shares jumped $13.39 to $74.11. Stock in Allergan added 77 cents to $298.79.Shares of the medical technology company Hill-Rom Holdings climbed 6.3 percent after it agreed to buy Welch Allyn, a privately held rival, for about $2.05 billion in a cash-and-stock deal. The stock gained $3.32 to $55.70.Shares of FedEx fell 3 percent after its latest quarterly results missed Wall Street’s expectations. The company said lower fuel surcharges and the strong dollar cut into revenue. FedEx reported a loss of $895 million, or $3.16 a share.In energy trading, the price of United States oil ended nearly flat on Wednesday after a volatile day of trading. Oil prices sagged earlier after the Energy Department’s weekly supply report showed a surprise increase in gasoline inventories.Benchmark United States crude oil fell 5 cents to settle at $59.92 a barrel in New York. Brent crude, a benchmark for international oil used by many United States refineries, rose 17 cents to $63.87 in London.In metals trading, gold prices slipped $4.10, or 0.3 percent, to $1,176 an ounce. In other futures trading, wholesale gasoline fell 2.4 cents to $2.101 a gallon. Heating oil rose 2.5 cents to $1.910 a gallon. Natural gas fell 3.9 cents to $2.855 per 1,000 cubic feet.Advertisement
