By THE ASSOCIATED PRESSDEC. 9, 2015
A slump in technology shares on Wednesday helped turn early gains in American stock indexes into losses across industries, extending the market’s losing streak to a third day.Investors sent the Dow Jones industrial average up 200 points in the morning, then began dumping big tech stocks. Apple fell 2.2 percent and Microsoft, 1.4 percent.By the end of the day, seven of the 10 industry sectors in the Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index fell. Suppliers of raw materials, the focus of aggressive selling recently, rose 3.1 percent as investors hunted for bargains.“You’ve got two days of massive selling of oil and commodity companies, so perhaps some are oversold,” said Ernest E. Cecilia, chief investment officer of Bryn Mawr Trust Wealth Management.AdvertisementHe added, though, that stock prices were not cheap enough yet for him to join in the buying.The Dow fell 75.70 points, or 0.4 percent, to 17,492.30. The S.&P. 500 gave up 15.97 points, or 0.8 percent, to 2,047.62. The Nasdaq dropped 75.38 points, or 1.5 percent, to 5,022.87.

Yahoo slumped after it said it would scrap a planned spinoff of its big stake in the Chinese e-commerce giant Alibaba. Yahoo stock lost 45 cents, or 1.3 percent, to $34.40. Yahoo said it would instead explore breaking off the rest of its business into a new company.AdvertisementSome of the biggest gainers were stocks that had big losses the day before. Freeport McMoRan, a miner that fell 7 percent on Tuesday, rose 3.7 percent, gaining 25 cents to close at $6.99.Helping raw material stocks were reports that two giant chemical companies, Dow Chemical and DuPont, were in talks to combine. Dow Chemical rose $6.07, or 11.9 percent, to $56.97. DuPont climbed $7.89, or 11.8 percent, to $74.49.Oil drilling companies, which have been beaten down recently, also rallied. Exxon Mobil and Chevron each rose 1.3 percent, despite another drop in the price of oil.Bill Strazzullo, chief market strategist at Bell Curve Trading, said the slump in oil and other commodities signaled that the world economy was weak.That could mean more rocky days in the market. “You got commodities in a death spiral, and that’s just not impacting the U.S., but the global economy,” he said. “Investors are taking off risk.”

Among other stocks making big moves Wednesday, Costco fell $9.15, or 5 percent, to $159.72, after reporting weaker earnings than analysts were expecting.AdvertisementThe company also said that sales at stores open at least a year, considered a key measure of a retailer’s health, fell 1 percent in the quarter. It said falling gasoline prices and unfavorable foreign exchange rates were to blame for the drop. Excluding those factors, sales at stores open a year would have risen 6 percent. Its shares were up more than 12 percent since the beginning of the year.Benchmark American crude slipped 35 cents to $37.16 a barrel in New York. Brent crude, the international standard, fell 15 cents to close at $40.11 a barrel in London.
