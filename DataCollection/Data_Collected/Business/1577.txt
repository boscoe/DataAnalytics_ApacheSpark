By THE ASSOCIATED PRESSNOV. 25, 2015
Stocks wobbled on Wednesday and finished little changed before Thanksgiving.The market was positive for most of the day, but its gains dissipated in the final minutes of trading. Last week, the markets made their biggest weekly jump of 2015, but since then the indexes have hardly budged.The Dow Jones industrial average rose 1.20 points to 17,813.39, while the Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index lost 0.27 points, or 0.01 percent, to 2,088.87. The Nasdaq composite index picked up 13.33 points, or 0.3 percent, to 5,116.14.Markets will be closed Thursday for Thanksgiving. They will reopen Friday, but will close at 1 p.m.A rebound by the drug companies Pfizer and Allergan elevated the health care sector, while consumer stocks like Amazon.com and Home Depot rose in the last trading day before Black Friday, which unofficially starts the holiday shopping season.Pfizer rose 90 cents, or 2.8 percent, to $32.87 and its Irish counterpart Allergan added $8.83, or 2.8 percent, to $320.26. On Monday, the companies said they would merge in a deal valued at about $155 billion. Pfizer shares fell a combined 10 percent Monday and Tuesday, and Allergan shares slipped after the deal was announced.

The agricultural equipment maker Deere rose after its fourth-quarter results and projections for the year were better than analysts expected. While sales of tractors, bulldozers and other machinery have dropped, Deere has slashed costs. The stock rose $3.66, or 4.8 percent, to $80.AdvertisementHewlett-Packard reported earnings for the final time Tuesday night, weeks after formally splitting into two companies. HP, which inherited the parent company’s personal computer, printer and commercial software business, sank $2, or 13.6 percent, to $12.64 as sales weakened.AdvertisementHewlett Packard Enterprise gained 43 cents, or 3.1 percent, to $14.12. Sales of hardware for data centers, including servers and networking devices, improved.Hormel, which makes Spam, said it was planning a stock split that would double the number of shares on the market, which could increase demand. Its shares rose $2.23, or 3 percent, to $73.55. The stock gained 3 percent Tuesday after the company reported a strong fourth quarter and outlook, and it has been trading at record highs.Energy stocks, which surged on Tuesday, gave back some of their gains, though the price of oil increased slightly. The government said crude oil stockpiles rose last week; they are at their highest levels in at least 80 years. The price of American crude rose 17 cents, or 0.4 percent, to $43.04 a barrel in New York. Brent crude, a benchmark for international oils, picked up 5 cents, to $46.17 a barrel in London.

Wholesale gasoline rose 0.6 cent, to $1.396 a gallon. Heating oil inched up 0.3 cent, to $1.403 a gallon. Natural gas rose 0.6 cent, to $2.206 per 1,000 cubic feet.The Commerce Department said consumer spending inched up 0.1 percent in October, the second small gain in a row. Despite weak spending in September and October, economists predict spending will keep growing because the labor market is strong.Spending on long-lasting manufactured goods improved in October after two months of declines. A measure of business investment improved by the biggest amount since July.Metals prices slipped after making gains on Tuesday. Gold declined $3.80, or 0.4 percent, to $1,070 an ounce. Silver was down a small fraction of a cent to $14.158 an ounce. Copper fell 0.9 cent, or 0.4 percent, to $2.046 a pound.Bond prices rose, and the yield on the 10-year Treasury inched down to 2.23 percent from 2.24 percent. The euro fell to $1.0617 from $1.0655 on Tuesday.
