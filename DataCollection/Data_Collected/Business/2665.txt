By THE ASSOCIATED PRESSJAN. 15, 2015
Disappointing results from Bank of America and Citigroup on Thursday tugged Wall Street to its fifth straight daily fall, as oil prices continued their slide and government bonds jumped.Weak revenue from trading helped pull down Bank of America’s profit 11 percent in the fourth quarter. The bank’s earnings and revenue fell short of analysts’ expectations. The company’s stock slid 84 cents, or 5.2 percent, to $15.20.Discouraging news on the global economy and falling oil prices have rattled investors recently, even as the bull market for stocks approaches its sixth anniversary. But Henry B. Smith, chief investment officer at the Haverford Trust Company, said the stock market’s fall was probably going to be temporary, another pause in a long climb.“Bull markets don’t die because of age,” Mr. Smith said. “They die almost always in anticipation of the next recession. But where are the indications of that?”AdvertisementDespite slowing growth overseas, the American economy continues to improve. Last week, the government said that the unemployment rate declined to 5.6 percent in December, a six-year low. On Thursday, the New York branch of the Federal Reserve reported that manufacturing had expanded in the region.AdvertisementJack A. Ablin, chief investment officer of BMO Private Bank, pointed to a conflict between the economy and the markets.

The Dow Jones industrial average dropped 106.38 points, or 0.6 percent, to 17,320.71, while the Nasdaq fell 68.50 points, or 1.5 percent, to 4,570.82.A volatile day of oil trading ended with crude falling $2.23 to $46.73 a barrel. Earlier in the day it jumped to more than $51. In London, Brent crude, a benchmark for international oils, fell by $1.02 to close at $47.67.Mr. Smith of Haverford Trust said he wasn’t troubled by the drop in oil prices. “Lower oil prices are good for the vast majority of the economy,” he said. “It can’t be bad because consumers in the U.S. and globally benefit from lower energy costs.”With JPMorgan Chase posting a drop in profits on Wednesday, the fourth-quarter earnings season has had a rough start. But that should not come as a surprise. Analysts have spent the past few weeks trimming their forecasts. They now predict that large corporations will report earnings growth of, on average, 4 percent, according to S&P Capital IQ, a research firm, down from forecasts of 6.6 percent on Dec. 1. Overall sales are expected to rise just 2.1 percent, largely because of sliding revenue for oil companies.Citigroup’s shares fell by $1.82, or 3.7 percent, to $47.23 after the company said its quarterly profit dropped 86 percent. Citigroup booked legal and restructuring charges at the end of last year to cover costs tied to several investigations. Analysts had expected stronger results.AdvertisementIn Europe, France’s CAC 40 rose 2.4 percent, while Germany’s DAX gained 2.2 percent. Britain’s FTSE 100 rose 1.7 percent.
