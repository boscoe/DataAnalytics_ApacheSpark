By THE ASSOCIATED PRESSNOV. 22, 2016
The Dow Jones industrial average surpassed 19,000 for the first time on Tuesday as a postelection rally on Wall Street drove indexes further into record territory. Discount store chains made large gains, but health care companies tumbled.Stocks opened solidly higher after setting records on Monday. They gave up some of their gains around midday but reached new highs late in the afternoon.The Dow picked up 67.18 points, or 0.4 percent, to close at 19,023.87. The Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index added 4.76 points, or 0.2 percent, closing at 2,202.94. The Nasdaq composite gained 17.49 points, or 0.3 percent, to close at 5,386.35.The Russell 2000 index, which tracks smaller companies, continued to set records as it traded higher for the 13th day in a row. It jumped 0.9 percent.

The Dow has closed at a record high six times in the two weeks since the presidential election, but trading volume has fallen in recent days.AdvertisementShoppers continued to flock to discount stores. Dollar Tree raised its profit and sales forecasts after the chain reported solid results in the third quarter. Burlington Stores also raised its outlook after it posted a larger profit than analysts expected. Dollar Tree jumped $6.69, or 8.2 percent, to $88.68, and Burlington Stores added $11.86, or 16 percent, to close at $86.04.AdvertisementHealth care stocks, which are still trading lower than they were at the start of this year, took hefty losses after weak results from Medtronic, one of the world’s largest medical device companies.Matt Miksic, a medical device analyst for UBS, said some investors worried that Medtronic’s results meant many drug and medical device companies would face slower growth. Mr. Miksic said Medtronic reported weak sales “across pretty much every one of their categories in the U.S.”The company also cut its profit guidance. It sank $6.98, or 8.7 percent, to $73.60.Campbell Soup’s profit in its fiscal first quarter was better than expected thanks to lower expenses and better sales of snack lines like Pepperidge Farm. Hormel Foods, the maker of Spam, reported better results from its refrigerated foods business and its Jennie-O turkey unit. Campbell Soup gained $1.98, or 3.6 percent, closing at $57.02, and Hormel rose 92 cents, or 2.6 percent, to $35.86.

Dr Pepper Snapple Group said it would buy the fruit drink maker Bai Brands for $1.7 billion. Bai Brands markets its drinks as having fewer calories than other brands and does not use artificial sweeteners. Dr Pepper Snapple stock picked up $2.25, or 2.6 percent, to close at $87.50.Oil prices wobbled and energy companies fell. Benchmark crude lost 21 cents to close at $48.03 a barrel in New York. Brent crude, the international standard, rose 22 cents to $49.12 a barrel in London. The price of oil rose about 4 percent Monday.Investors continued to sell short-term bonds, which sent their prices lower. The yield on the two-year Treasury note rose to 1.09 percent, its highest in six years. Longer-term bond prices held steady.
