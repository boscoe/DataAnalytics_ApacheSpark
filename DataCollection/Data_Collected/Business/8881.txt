By THE ASSOCIATED PRESSSEPT. 11, 2015
Major stock indexes shook off an early stumble on Friday — falling oil prices pulled oil and gas companies down — to finish the day with slight gains as traders turned their attention to a pivotal meeting of the Federal Reserve next week.It was a quiet end to another turbulent week. Thanks largely to a big jump on Tuesday, the United States market finished with a 2 percent gain for the week, recouping a portion of the steep losses from the week before.“It seems people are focused on the market’s volatility and the potential impact of a slowing China,” said Philip J. Orlando, chief equity strategist at Federated Investors in New York, the money management firm. “I understand why folks are nervous. I think eventually things will settle down.”The Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index gained 8.76 points, or 0.5 percent, to close at 1,961.05. The Dow Jones industrial average rose 102.69 points, or 0.6 percent, to close at 16,433.09. The Nasdaq rose 26.09, or 0.5 percent, to close at 4,822.34.AdvertisementNews about China’s slowing economy, a looming rate increase from the Fed and a host of other concerns have combined to knock the market down 6 percent over the last month. It has been a staggered fall, with sharp drops one week followed by slight gains the next.AdvertisementWall Street is divided over whether the Fed will raise its benchmark lending rate next week for the first time in nine years. The Fed reduced its key rate to near zero during the financial crisis, supporting the stock market’s seven-year run. Uncertainty over the Fed’s timing has kept investors on edge.

Major markets in Europe closed with losses on Friday. The DAX in Germany dropped 0.9 percent, while the CAC-40 in France sank 1 percent. The FTSE 100 in Britain slipped 0.6 percent.In China, the Shanghai Composite Index added 0.1 percent. In Hong Kong, the Hang Seng fell 0.3 percent. In Japan, the Nikkei 225 fell 0.2 percent.Back in the United States, Kroger, the grocery store chain, gained 5.3 percent after reporting earnings that beat analysts’ estimates.
