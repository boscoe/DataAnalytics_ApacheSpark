By THE ASSOCIATED PRESSJAN. 21, 2015
Another choppy day on Wall Street ended with shares broadly higher on hopes of new stimulus measures for Europe’s weak economy and a sharp rise in oil prices.Stocks flitted between gains and losses at the open of trading on Wednesday, then rose on news media reports that new stimulus measures by the European Central Bank would be as large as investors anticipated. The bank is expected on Thursday to unveil an extensive round of government bond-buying, a program known as quantitative easing.All 10 sectors of the Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index rose. A gain in oil prices helped push the energy sector up 1.8 percent, the biggest gainer.The bumpy market is not surprising after big stock gains the last two years, said Philip Orlando, chief equity strategist at Federated Investments.Advertisement“Investors are worried the gains can’t possibly last another year,” he said.Investors also weighed a batch of corporate earnings reports. Netflix shares surged 17 percent on a jump in fourth-quarter profits, but IBM’s results disappointed and its stock dropped 3 percent.AdvertisementEurope is facing anemic growth, high unemployment and falling prices. To combat this, many investors had been expecting the European Central Bank to buy 500 billion euros ($580 billion) of various government bonds.“All eyes are on Mario Draghi,” said Anastasia Amoroso, global market strategist at J.P. Morgan Asset Management, referring to the central bank’s president.The S.&P. 500 rose 9.57 points, or 0.5 percent, to close at 2,032.12. It was third consecutive day of gains for the index, a first in the new year. The Dow Jones industrial average climbed 39.05 points, or 0.2 percent, to 17,554.28. The Nasdaq gained 12.57 points, or 0.3 percent, to 4,667.42.Reporting of fourth-quarter corporate earnings is in full swing. When all S.&P. 500 companies have reported, earnings per share are expected to edge up 0.5 percent, the smallest quarterly gain in two years, according to FactSet. A slump in oil, down more than 50 percent over the last seven months, is largely to blame. Earnings per share at energy companies are expected to fall 22 percent from a year earlier.
