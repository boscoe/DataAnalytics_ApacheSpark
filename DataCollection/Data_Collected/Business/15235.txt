AdvertisementSupported byBy The Associated PressStocks on Wall Street jumped to record highs Friday after a strong jobs report for the month of July gave investors more confidence that the economy was still growing. Financial and technology stocks soared, and investors sold the safe assets they have favored for most of this year.The Labor Department said United States employers added 255,000 jobs in July, far more than investors expected and the second straight month of strong gains after shaky reports this spring.Stocks made their biggest gain in almost a month. Banks traded higher as investors anticipated higher interest rates and bigger profits on mortgages and other loans. The Nasdaq composite closed at a record high as tech stocks continued to climb. Consumer companies also made big gains. Investors sold bonds, precious metals, and phone and utility companies, safe investments that soared earlier this year as investors worried about the health of the economy.“It looks like the economy is improving, it looks like corporate earnings are on the upswing,” said Sam Stovall, United States equity strategist for S&P Capital IQ. That marks a change from earlier this year, he said, when investors worried the economy would fall into a recession and tech companies would suffer as businesses cut spending. But tech stocks have come back to lead the market higher over the last few months.The Dow Jones industrial average rose 191.48 points, or 1 percent, to 18,543.53. The Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index climbed 18.62 points, or 0.9 percent, to 2,182.87. The Nasdaq composite index advanced 54.87 points, or 1.1 percent, to 5,221.12.Banks rose the most, as higher interest rates help their profits from lending. Bank of America rose 56 cents, or 3.9 percent, to $15.05, and Citigroup added $1.88, or 4.3 percent, to $45.72.The Federal Reserve has been saying for months that it intends to raise interest rates if the economy’s strength warrants it. July’s report provides more evidence the economy is doing well, boosting the chances that interest rates will go up.Bond prices fell and the yield on the 10-year Treasury note jumped to 1.59 percent from 1.50 percent.Kraft Heinz, the company behind Oscar Mayer bologna, Jell-O pudding and Velveeta cheese, also traded higher after it reported a larger profit than analysts expected. Its stock picked up 3.8 percent.After Bristol-Myers Squibb said one of its cancer drugs failed in a clinical trial, its stock suffered its biggest slump since 2000, falling 16 percent. Merck, which makes a rival drug that now stands to gain market share, soared 10.4 percent. Merck was the largest gainer among S.&P. 500 stocks, and Bristol-Myers Squibb took the largest loss.Benchmark crude slid 13 cents to $41.80 a barrel in New York. Brent crude, used to price international oils, rose 13 cents to $44.42 a barrel in London.The price of gold fell $22.40, to $1,336.40 an ounce. Silver fell 72 cents, or 3.5 percent, to $19.73 an ounce. Copper lost 2 cents to $2.15 a pound.In other energy trading, wholesale gasoline held steady at $1.38 a gallon. Heating oil lost 1 cent to $1.32 a gallon. Natural gas fell 6 cents to $2.77 per 1,000 cubic feet.The dollar rose to 101.74 yen, from 101.16 yen, and the euro fell to $1.1086, from $1.1129.European stocks also advanced. In France, the CAC 40 rose 1.5 percent and the DAX in Germany advanced 1.4 percent. The British FTSE 100 was 0.8 percent higher. In Asia, China’s Shanghai Composite Index shed 0.2 percent and Japan’s Nikkei 225 was little changed. The South Korean Kospi rose 0.9 percent.Advertisement
