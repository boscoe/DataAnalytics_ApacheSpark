AdvertisementSupported byBy The Associated PressBig media companies led the stock market lower on Thursday as investors fretted over fading revenue from cable television. Viacom and 21st Century Fox were among the hardest hit.Major indexes increased in the first few minutes of trading before quickly turning down. The indexes recovered some of their losses after selling gained momentum until the afternoon.Shares in Walt Disney, along with other media giants’ stocks, sank after signs emerged that more people were canceling their cable TV. Viacom — the company behind Comedy Central, Nickelodeon and MTV — reported Thursday that its sales and profit fell in the most recent quarter; 21st Century Fox also reported a drop in television revenue. Viacom’s stock plunged 14.2 percent, and 21st Century Fox lost 6.7 percent.“You don’t usually see media names move like this,” said Rob Eschweiler, a global investment specialist at J.P. Morgan Private Bank in Houston.The Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index fell 16.28 points, or 0.8 percent, to 2,083.56, and the Nasdaq composite lost 83.5, or 1.6 percent, to 5,056.44. The Dow Jones industrial average lost 120.72, or 0.7 percent, to 17,419.75, the sixth day in a row the Dow has closed with a loss.Over the last month, the market has been making gains one week and losing them all the next. “We’ve been moving nowhere fast,” said Terry Sandven, senior equity strategist at U.S. Bank Wealth Management in Minneapolis. “The market just lacks any direction.”Mr. Sandven said he thought things would change once investors got a clear picture of how quickly the Federal Reserve would raise interest rates for the first time in more than nine years. The Fed is expected to make its first move in September.“It will mean that the Fed thinks the economy is strong enough to handle something other than crisis-level rates,” Mr. Sandven said.Among other companies in the news, Keurig Green Mountain shares plummeted nearly 30 percent, the biggest drop in the S.&P. 500, after the company reported falling sales of its packaged coffee and brewing products. The company said it planned to lay off 5 percent of its work force in a bid to cut costs. Its stock dropped $22.31 to $52.67.With the bulk of big companies already handing in results, analysts project that second-quarter earnings at big United States companies edged up 0.2 percent, according to S&P Capital IQ. Though meager, it’s much better than the 4 percent drop analysts had forecast a month ago.Investors are looking ahead to the Labor Department’s monthly jobs report on Friday. According to economists’ forecasts, the government report will show that employers added 225,000 jobs and that the unemployment rate held at 5.3 percent for the second straight month. That level of job creation would buttress expectations that the Fed will lift its benchmark interest rate this year.“I think if the Fed doesn’t move this year, it’s going to be a disappointment,” Mr. Eschweiler said. “It would be a bit of a head scratcher. What do they know that we don’t?”Major markets in Europe ended with slight losses. Germany’s DAX lost 0.4 percent, while France’s CAC 40 and Britain’s FTSE 100 slipped 0.1 percent.In Asia, Japan’s benchmark Nikkei 225 inched up 0.2 percent while South Korea’s Kospi lost 0.8 percent. Hong Kong’s Hang Seng fell 0.6 percent, and the Shanghai Composite fell 0.9 percent. Benchmarks in Taiwan, Indonesia, the Philippines and New Zealand also finished lower.In the United States, government bond prices rose, pushing the yield on the 10-year Treasury note down to 2.22 percent from 2.27 percent on Wednesday. The price fell 12/32, to 99 4/32.In commodity trading, gold rose $4.50 to $1,090.10 an ounce, and silver gained 12 cents to $14.68. Copper lost a penny to $2.34 a pound.The price of oil fell near its low for the year as a Goldman Sachs report predicting that oil prices would be “lower for longer” reinforced concerns that had driven oil lower over the last six weeks. United States crude fell 49 cents to settle at $44.66 a barrel in New York. Brent crude, a benchmark for international oils used by many American refineries, fell 7 cents to $49.52 in London.Advertisement
