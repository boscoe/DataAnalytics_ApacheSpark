
UPDATED July 10, 2015

European leaders weighed the latest measures from Athens aimed at overhauling its economy and securing its place in the euro currency bloc.
Related Article
Why it matters: It is running out of money and struggling to repay billions in debt it owes to international creditors.“We have a mandate from the Greek people to bring a better agreement. We don’t have a mandate to take the country out of the eurozone. Either we all stay together or we all leave together.”

        
         Alexis Tsipras, Greece’s prime minister, addressing Syriza lawmakers, emphasizing that his priority was to seal a deal.
      What’s next: Mr. Tsipras said that the “chances for a deal and another bailout for Greece have increased again.” The government’s latest proposal for a bailout to help it stay in the eurozone is being reviewed by lawmakers and the country’s creditors.Why it matters: An influential creditor that has largely stayed in line with Germany’s position.“The Greek program is serious, credible and shows a determination to remain in the eurozone. Nothing is done yet, everything can be done.”

        
         President François Hollande welcomed Greece’s policy overhauls, which French officials helped draft.
      What’s next: A European Union official, who spoke on the condition of anonymity because the plans were evolving, suggested that an agreement could, in theory, be reached on Saturday at a meeting of eurozone finance ministers.Why it matters: The most powerful of Greece’s exasperated creditors.“The European currency and the eurozone are much more than just the question of who shares the same money. But it only has a future if we all stick to the rules, the ones agreed in advance.”
        
         Steffen Seibert, a government spokesman. Chancellor Angela Merkel and other top German ministers were quiet on Friday, saying it was too soon to judge the proposals.
      What’s next: The proposals are being reviewed by the European Commission, the European Central Bank and the International Monetary Fund.Why it matters: Any resumption of bailout talks would probably need a green light from this group of the finance ministers of member countries.“Broad support in Greece gives it more credibility, but even then we need to consider carefully whether the proposal is good and if the numbers add up.”

        
         Jeroen Dijsselbloem, the Dutch finance minister, who is president of the Eurogroup, according to Reuters.
      What’s next: The Eurogroup will meet in Brussels on Saturday afternoon to consider negotiations.Why it matters: A group of heads of state of European Union member states that directs European Union policy.Any “realistic proposal from Athens needs to be matched by realistic proposal from creditors on debt sustainability to create win-win situation.”

        
         Donald Tusk, a former prime minister of Poland and the president of the European Council, said on Twitter.
