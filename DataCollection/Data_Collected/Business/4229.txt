AdvertisementSupported byBy The Associated PressThe stock market’s bull run turned six years old on Monday. Modest gains marked the anniversary.Stocks were rebounding from a big sell-off on Friday when the market slumped after an unexpectedly strong jobs report. The healthy hiring picture bolstered speculation that the Federal Reserve will raise its interest rates sooner than had been expected.On Monday, General Motors was among the biggest gainers after announcing a $5 billion stock buyback plan. Deal news also helped the market. Macerich, a real estate investment trust, jumped after a hostile bid from Simon Property, a bigger rival. Stocks are becoming more volatile as investors try to assess when the Federal Reserve will start to raise interest rates and how that will affect the economy. The Fed has kept its benchmark lending rate close to zero for six years, underpinning the run in stocks that began in March 2009.“Every time the market settles in on what the Fed will do, it gets spooked,” said James P. Dunigan, chief investment officer at PNC Asset Management. “Today, that anxiety subsided a bit.”The Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index rose 8.17 points, or 0.4 percent, to 2,079.43. The Dow Jones industrial average gained 138.94 points, or 0.8 percent, to 17,995.72. The Nasdaq composite index climbed 15.07 points, or 0.3 percent, to 4,942.44. G.M. was among the biggest gainers on Monday after it announced a plan to buy its own stock. The move is part of a deal with Harry Wilson, an activist investor and a former member of the government task force that restructured G.M. as it came out of its 2009 bankruptcy. In exchange, Mr. Wilson agreed to withdraw his hostile candidacy for G.M.’s board. The company’s stock rose $1.12, or 3.1 percent, to $37.66. Macerich, a real estate investment trust that specializes in retail properties, was another winner. Its stock climbed $6.04, or 7 percent, to $92.76 after Simon Property made a hostile bid of $16 billion in cash and stock for the company.The S.&P. 500 has tripled since bottoming out at 676.53 six years ago after the housing collapse and the Great Recession. The streak of gains is the fourth-longest since the 1940s and has pushed the stock market to record highs. But few analysts see an end to the bull market yet.Scott L. Wren, a senior global equity strategist at the Wells Fargo Investment Institute, said investors should still take advantage of any sell-offs to add to their positions.“Over all, you’re still going to get out of this year with good, but not great, returns,” he said, predicting that growth abroad will pick up, helping to underpin a steady recovery in the United States.Industrial and technology stocks led gains on Monday. These so-called cyclical stocks are most likely to reap the greatest benefit if economic growth increases.The dollar traded at $1.0853 against the euro, close to a 12-year high. It also gained against the yen, climbing to 121.13 yen. United States government bond prices rose. The yield on the 10-year Treasury note fell to 2.19 percent from 2.24 percent Friday.Precious and industrial metals futures closed mixed. Gold edged up $2.30 to $1,166.40 an ounce, silver fell 3 cents to $15.78 an ounce and copper rose 6 cents to $2.67 a pound.The price of oil rose slightly on forecasts that a substantial increase in inventories at the country’s main storage hub in Cushing, Okla., may be abating.Benchmark United States crude rose 39 cents to close at $50 a barrel in New York. Brent crude, a benchmark for international oils used by many United States refineries, fell $1.20 to close at $58.53 a barrel in London.In other futures trading on the Nymex, wholesale gasoline fell 0.7 cent to close at $1.875 a gallon; heating oil fell 2.9 cents to close at $1.840 a gallon; and natural gas fell 16.1 cents to close at $2.678 per 1,000 cubic feet.Advertisement
