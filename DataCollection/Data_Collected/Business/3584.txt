AdvertisementSupported byBy The Associated PressA slight gain was enough to push the stock market to a new closing high on Friday.Stocks climbed as a rebound in oil prices helped energy stocks move higher. A report that showed growth had picked up faster than forecast in Europe in the fourth quarter of last year seemed to lift investor sentiment.Investors were also poring over the latest earnings news. CBS gained after reporting earnings that exceeded analysts’ expectations, as the media company received a lift from better advertising revenue. The V. F. Corporation, a clothing company whose brands include Vans, Wrangler and Timberland, rose after giving an upbeat outlook for the year.Stocks have surged in February, rebounding from a January slump as recovering oil prices raised energy stocks. Growing corporate earnings and the announcement of more economic stimulus in Europe have also helped turn investor sentiment around this month.J. J. Kinahan, chief strategist at TD Ameritrade, said, “Over all, I think the market is going to go higher,” adding that “it may be a case of two steps forward, one step back.”The Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index rose 8.51 points, or 0.4 percent, to 2,096.99. That surpassed the previous high close of 2,090.57 set Dec. 29.The Dow Jones industrial average climbed 46.97 points, or 0.3 percent, to 18,019.35. The index is still 35 points short of its high. The Nasdaq gained 36.22 points, or 0.8 percent, to 4,893.84.About three-quarters of the companies in the S.&P. 500 index have reported results for the fourth quarter, and earnings for the period are projected to rise by 7.5 percent. While that is a decline from growth of 10.4 percent in the previous quarter, it is better than analysts were expecting at the start of December.On Friday, the V. F. Corporation was one of the biggest gainers in the S.&P. 500. The company gained $4.26, or 6 percent, to $75.26 after it said that it was expecting “meaningful growth” in all of its markets worldwide, despite challenges posed by a strengthening dollar.CBS was another winner. The company gained $2.06, or 3.6 percent, to $59.83 after it reported earnings late Thursday that were slightly better than Wall Street analysts had expected. The company was helped by higher advertising revenue, led by the broadcast of “Thursday Night Football” and political ad revenue associated with the midterm elections.This month’s sharp gains are making some analysts cautious on stocks. The price-earnings ratio for next year’s earnings for S.&P. 500 companies is at 17.1, the highest level in more than a decade. The measure is a gauge of how much investors are willing to pay for a company’s earnings.“Watch those valuation levels very carefully,” said James Liu, Global Market Strategist for J. P. Morgan Asset Management.Rather than tracking the broader market, investors should focus on certain sectors, Mr. Liu says. At the moment, he favors so-called consumer discretionary stocks, which should benefit as hiring picks up and consumers have more money in their pockets from lower gas prices.ConAgra was one of the biggest losers in the S.&P. 500 on Friday. The company, whose brands include Swiss Miss hot chocolate mix and Slim Jim beef jerky, dropped $1.59, or 4.4 percent, to $34.83 after cutting its earnings outlook for the year late Thursday. ConAgra blamed the impact of a stronger dollar and intense competition for its Private Brands unit.While energy stocks have been rebounding this year, one of last year’s biggest gainers is this year’s biggest decliner.Utilities surged 25 percent last year as investors pushed up the price of the dividend-rich stocks in the face of falling bond yields. Now, as bond yields are showing signs of rising from their lows, investors are dumping utilities. The sector is down 7 percent this month.American stocks again got a lift from developments in Europe. Data out Friday showed that the eurozone economy picked up speed in the fourth quarter thanks to better growth in Germany and Spain. The currency union’s economy grew 0.3 percent in the October-to-December period from the previous quarter, more than expected, thanks also to lower oil prices and a weaker euro. The growth rate, while encouraging, is only about half that of the United States.Greece and its creditors in the 19-country eurozone took visible, if modest, steps to bridge their differences over Greek demands to lighten the load of its financial bailout. Investors are hopeful that a deal will be reached to avoid Greece’s exit from the euro.In energy trading, benchmark United States crude rose $1.57, to $52.78 a barrel, on the New York Mercantile Exchange. Brent crude climbed $2.24, to $61.52 a barrel, in London.In bond trading, the price of the 10-year Treasury note dropped 19/32, to 99 17/32. Its yield rose to 2.05 percent from 1.99 percent on Thursday.The dollar was little changed against the Japanese yen and the euro. The dollar traded at 118.75 yen, down from 118.85 yen the previous day. The euro was flat at $1.1406.In metals trading, precious and industrial metals futures closed higher. Gold rose $6.40, to $1,227.10 an ounce, silver rose 50 cents, to $17.29 an ounce; and copper edged up less than a penny, to $2.61 a pound.In other energy futures trading on the Nymex, wholesale gasoline rose 3 cents to close at $1.626 a gallon; heating oil rose 5.7 cents to close at $1.971 a gallon; and natural gas rose 9.1 cents to close at $2.804 per 1,000 cubic feet.Advertisement
