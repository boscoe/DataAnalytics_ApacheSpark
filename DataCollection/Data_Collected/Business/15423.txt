By THE ASSOCIATED PRESSAUG. 17, 2016
Stocks on Wall Street closed barely higher on Wednesday as big gains for utilities balanced out losses for retailers like Lowe’s, Target and Staples.Stocks fell in morning trading as a recent slump in phone company and utility shares continued. But the indexes reversed direction after noon as those stocks turned higher, as did those of banks and household goods makers. Investors scrutinized the minutes from the Federal Reserve’s late July meeting and found no suggestion that the central bank was in any hurry to raise interest rates.The Dow Jones industrial average rose 21.92 points, or 0.1 percent, to 18,573.94. The Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index gained 4.07 points, or 0.2 percent, to 2,182.22 after falling as much as 10 points early on. The Nasdaq composite inched up 1.55 points to 5,228.66. That left the market little changed from Tuesday and continued a persistent pattern of small moves for United States stocks.In the meeting minutes, Federal Reserve officials said near-term risks to the economy diminished as job growth improved in June and July. It said an increase in interest rates might be warranted before long, but investors doubt that will happen in September and are unsure if it will happen in the months after that.AdvertisementUtility companies had the biggest gains, as low interest rates and bond yields made their big dividend payments more appealing. Dominion Resources rose 2.6 percent, and Xcel Energy added 1.7 percent.AdvertisementConsumer companies slumped after weak results and forecasts for some major retailers. Lowe’s cut its annual forecast after it reported a profit that was smaller than analysts expected, and sales at older stores were weak. Lowe’s stock fell 5.7 percent. Target also lowered its profit projections; its stock lost 6.4 percent.Staples fell after disappointing analysts with its forecasts, which included further sales declines. The company said sales at older stores improved, surprising analysts. The stock gained 15.4 percent. It is up 58 percent this year, wiping out a steep loss from 2015.Barnes & Noble tumbled after the bookseller said its chief executive, Ronald Boire, would leave after less than a year in the job. The company said its board had determined that Mr. Boire was not a good fit. Leonard Riggio, chairman and former chief executive, who was scheduled to retire next month, will stay with the company as it seeks a new chief. The stock sank 11 percent.Bond prices turned higher and the yield on the 10-year Treasury note fell to 1.56 percent from 1.58 percent. The dollar weakened, falling to 100.22 yen from 100.26 yen. The euro rose to $1.1279 from $1.1277. In recent days, the Fed’s decision to leave rates unchanged has weakened the dollar, helping exporters.Oil prices climbed after the Energy Information Administration said crude oil inventories shrank by 2.5 million barrels last week and gas stockpiles decreased by 2.7 million barrels. The declines were larger than expected, which is generally good for oil prices. Benchmark crude oil added 21 cents, to $46.79 a barrel, in New York. Brent crude, used to price international oils, inched up 62 cents, or 1.3 percent, to $49.85 a barrel in London.The price of gold fell $1.57 to $1,348.93 an ounce. Silver fell 23 cents, or 1.1 percent, to $19.70 an ounce. Copper gave up 2 cents to $2.16 a pound.In France, the CAC 40 dropped 1 percent, while the DAX in Germany shed 1.3 percent. The FTSE 100 in London declined 0.5 percent. The Hang Seng fell 0.5 percent in Hong Kong, and the Nikkei 225 in Tokyo gained 0.9 percent.
