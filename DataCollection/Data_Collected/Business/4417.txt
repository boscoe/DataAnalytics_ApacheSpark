AdvertisementSupported byBy The Associated PressStocks bounced back on Monday, after losing ground for three weeks, as the dollar’s rally against the euro abated.Elsewhere in financial markets, oil settled at a six-year low, below $44 a barrel, as supplies continue to outpace demand. Treasurys gained after some mixed reports on the economy.The stock market has stumbled in recent weeks as the dollar has surged against the euro. The American currency has been rising on expectations that the Federal Reserve will start to raise interest rates even as the European Central Bank expands its stimulus for that region’s economy.A stronger dollar is a problem for big companies in the United States that rely on overseas sales because it makes their goods more expensive in foreign markets and reduces the value of the profits they bring back home.“The dollar was weaker today, which was helpful,” said Quincy Krosby, a market strategist at Prudential Financial. “There’s no doubt that the stronger dollar has been impeding sales.”The Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index rose 27.79 points, or 1.4 percent, to 2,081.19. It was the biggest gain for the index in six weeks. The Dow Jones industrial average climbed 228.11 points, or 1.3 percent, to 17,977.42. The Nasdaq composite index jumped 57.75 points, or 1.2 percent, to 4,929.51.The focal point this week for investors is the Fed’s two-day policy meeting that starts on Tuesday. Many investors and analysts expect the central bank will signal in a statement after the meeting that it is considering raising interest rates this year. The Fed has kept its benchmark lending rate near zero for more than six years, underpinning a strong rally in stocks.Investors will also be looking for any comments Fed policy makers might make on the impact to the economy of the rapid surge in the dollar, Ms. Krosby said.Stocks rose broadly on Monday. Nine of the 10 industry groups that make up the S.&P. 500 index rose. The market gained despite some mixed economic reports. Industrial production in the United States edged up slightly in February as a big surge by utilities due to a cold winter offset a third straight decline in factory output. The Federal Reserve also reported that industrial production rose 0.1 percent in February after a 0.3 percent fall in January.Another report showed that American homebuilders were feeling slightly less confident in their sales prospects, even as their overall sales outlook remains favorable. The National Association of Home Builders/Wells Fargo builder sentiment index slipped this month to 53, down two points from February. It is the third monthly decline in a row.The economic data gave a lift to bond prices, pushing Treasury yields lower. The yield on the 10-year Treasury note dropped to 2.07 percent from 2.11 percent on Friday.Health care stocks were the biggest gainers of the S.&P.’s 10 industry groups, rising 2.2 percent. Gains for the sector were led by Edwards Lifesciences, a company that develops and manufactures products to treat heart disease. On Sunday, the company announced positive study results for its third-generation heart-valve replacement system.The company’s stock climbed $13.29, or 9.8 percent, to $148.64, making it the biggest gainer in the S.&P. 500.Avon Products was the biggest loser in the index. The company’s stock is being removed from the S.&P. 500 on Friday. It will be replaced by the men’s wear company Hanesbrands. Avon shares fell 44 cents, or 5.7 percent, to $7.28.Energy stocks climbed despite another big slump in the price of oil. Oil fell to its lowest level in six years on expectations that rising supplies in the United States are far outpacing demand. Benchmark United States crude oil fell 96 cents to settle at $43.88 a barrel in New York.The price of oil has dropped 12 percent over the past week, and is now the lowest since March 2009. Brent crude, a benchmark for international oils used by many American refineries, fell $1.23 to settle at $53.44 in London.In Europe, Germany’s DAX index rose 2.2 percent to 12,167.72, the first time the index has closed above 12,000. France’s CAC 40 rose 1 percent to 5,061.16, while Britain’s FTSE 100 rose 0.9 percent to 6,804.08. European stocks have surged this year after the European Central Bank announced that it would introduce more stimulus to revive the region’s slumping economy.In currency trading, the euro strengthened to $1.0574 from $1.0476 on Friday. The dollar has surged against the euro this year, and the strengthening dollar hurts American companies like Coca-Cola and Procter & Gamble that rely on overseas sales for a large chunk of their revenue.The dollar was little changed against the Japanese currency at 121.39 yen.Precious and industrial metals futures edged higher. Gold rose 80 cents to $1,153.20 an ounce, silver rose 12 cents to $15.62 an ounce, and copper inched up less than a penny to $2.67 a pound.In other futures trading on the Nymex, wholesale gasoline fell 3.3 cents to $1.729 a gallon, heating oil fell 1.4 cents to $1.699 a gallon, and natural gas fell 1.1 cents to $2.716 per 1,000 cubic feet.Advertisement
