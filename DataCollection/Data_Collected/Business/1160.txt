AdvertisementSupported byBusiness BriefingBy The Associated PressA federal bankruptcy judge has ruled that people suing General Motors over faulty ignition switches can seek punitive damages that could cost the automaker millions of dollars. When General Motors emerged from a 2009 bankruptcy, it became known as “New G.M.” and was essentially shielded from liabilities of the company that was left behind. But Judge Robert Gerber in New York ruled on Monday that employees and knowledge transferred from the “Old G.M.” to the new company. Plaintiffs, he ruled, can seek punitive damages if they can show that “New G.M.” knew of the defect but covered it up. Advertisement
