By THE ASSOCIATED PRESSJULY 28, 2015
Global stocks steadied and United States markets advanced on Tuesday as investors were encouraged by strong results from UPS, Ford and other big companies.It is a reversal of five straight days of losses for American indexes. It is not uncommon for stocks to change course after several days in one direction, and investors say the market is still lacking the foundation for an extended rally.“We just don’t see any significant catalyst that will move this market higher, even after last week’s declines,” said Kristina Hooper, global market strategist with Allianz Global Investors.The Dow Jones industrial average rose 189.68 points, or 1.1 percent, to 17,630.27, ending near its high for the day. The Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index was up 25.61 points, or 1.2 percent, to 2,093.25, and the Nasdaq composite index rose 49.43 points, or 1 percent, to 5,089.21.

A dose of corporate earnings gains helped drive the advance.Shares of UPS were up more than 5 percent. Overseas business helped the company during its second quarter, as did the absence of a hefty charge recorded a year ago. UPS earned $1.23 billion, or $1.35 a share, for the three months ended June 30. The results topped Wall Street’s expectations. The average estimate of 12 analysts surveyed by Zacks Investment Research was for $1.26 a share. A year earlier, UPS earned $454 million, or 49 cents a share.AdvertisementUPS is sometimes seen a proxy for the global economy because of its huge role in delivering goods all over the world on a daily basis.AdvertisementFord shares rose 28 cents, or 1.9 percent, to $14.83. The carmaker said profits jumped 44 percent in the second quarter, helped by higher global sales and higher prices for premium trucks and S.U.V.s. The profit of 47 cents a share easily beat Wall Street’s expectations of 37 cents a share.Global markets were a little less stressed on Tuesday. The Shanghai composite index closed down 1.7 percent, but had been trading down as much as 4 percent earlier in the day. Chinese stocks plunged 8.5 percent on Monday, the biggest drop since February 2007, despite concerted efforts by the Chinese government to stem the market’s slide. European stocks rose roughly 1 percent.Traders were turning their attention to the Federal Reserve Bank as they try to assess when interest rates will rise. Fed policy makers started a two-day meeting on Tuesday, but few central bank watchers expect a rate increase.

Many say they believe the Fed will begin its next cycle of rate increases in September or December. Ultra-low interest rates have been a boon for stock and bond markets, and many questions remain about how markets will react to the first increase since the 2008 financial crisis.In other markets, the price of United States crude rose for the first time in five days on expectations that supply reports this week could show a decline. Brent crude, a benchmark for international oils used by many American refineries, continued to fall, however. Benchmark United States crude rose 59 cents to settle at $47.98 a barrel in New York. Brent fell 17 cents to $53.30 a barrel in London.In other futures trading on the New York Mercantile Exchange, wholesale gasoline fell 1.7 cents to close at $1.803 a gallon; heating oil rose 0.8 cents to close at $1.604 a gallon; and natural gas rose 3.2 cents to close at $2.821 per 1,000 cubic feet.United States government bond prices fell. The yield on the 10-year Treasury note rose to 2.25 percent from 2.22 percent.The dollar rose to 123.56 Japanese yen from 123.26 yen on Monday.
