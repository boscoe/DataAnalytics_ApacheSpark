By THE ASSOCIATED PRESSMARCH 3, 2016
Stocks made modest gains on Thursday as the market again turned higher late in the day.Energy stocks led the way as investors continued to hope that oil prices have stabilized after almost two years of steep declines.For the second day in a row, stocks opened with small losses and gradually rose in the afternoon. Energy companies surged and are now slightly higher for the year. Industrial companies like Caterpillar and Deere also rose. Drug companies led a decline in health care stocks.The Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index edged up 6.95 points, or 0.4 percent, to 1,993.40. Technology stocks lagged, and the Nasdaq composite index added 4 points, or 0.1 percent, to 4,703.42.The Dow Jones industrial average gained 44.58 points, or 0.3 percent, to 16,943.90.Stocks have eked out small gains over the last two days, aided by steady oil prices and reports showing the United States economy is on solid footing. The market is on target for its third consecutive weekly gain.

The price of American crude wavered between small gains and losses, finally closing down 9 cents at $34.57 a barrel in New York. Brent crude, the benchmark for international oils, added 14 cents to $37.07 a barrel in London. The price of domestic oil has risen more than 30 percent in three weeks, and Brent crude has erased its losses for the year.AdvertisementConocoPhillips rose $2.07, or 5.7 percent, to $38.56 and Southwestern Energy jumped $1.13, or 18.2 percent, to $7.34.AdvertisementChesapeake Energy continued to skyrocket after it said it did not expect to be prosecuted or fined as part of a federal investigation into Aubrey McClendon, its founder and former chief, who died in a car accident on Wednesday. A federal grand jury had indicted Mr. McClendon on charges of rigging bids for gas leases.Shares of Chesapeake jumped 23 percent on Wednesday and added 87 cents, or 25.6 percent, to close at $4.27 on Thursday.JJ Kinahan, chief market strategist for TD Ameritrade, said investors seemed to be waiting for good news about the economy. That could come Friday morning, when the government reports its latest employment figures.

Mr. Kinahan said investors would be looking for signs of growth in better-paying jobs, possibly in manufacturing or health care, as opposed to restaurants and hotels.“We know we’re not going to be a manufacturing economy again,” he said, but investors hope to see some growth in manufacturing jobs instead of losses.The Commerce Department said factory orders grew 1.6 percent in January, the biggest gain in seven months. A category that measures business investment rose by the largest amount in 19 months.Joy Global, a mining equipment maker, climbed $2.77, or 20.8 percent, to $16.09 after its first-quarter sales were stronger than expected. Stratasys, which makes 3-D printers, rose $3.64, or 17.4 percent, to $24.53. The company’s fourth-quarter results were better than expected and it gave a strong forecast for 2016.AdvertisementKroger, a supermarket operator, dropped $2.85, or 7 percent, to $37.80 after investors were disappointed with its quarterly sales and forecasts.

Losses for biotech drug companies pulled health care stocks lower. Celgene, which makes cancer drugs, lost $2, to $102.73, and Gilead Sciences, which makes a drug for hepatitis C, fell 97 cents, to $87.83. Alexion Pharmaceuticals sank $5.74, or 3.8 percent, to $145.85.The prices of gold, silver and copper each rose about 1 percent. Gold rose $16.30, to $1,257.40 an ounce, and silver closed up 12 cents, at $15.15 an ounce. Copper advanced 3 cents, to $2.21 a pound. The price of gold has climbed almost 19 percent this year, and silver has risen about 10 percent.The FTSE 100 in Britain and DAX in Germany each fell 0.3 percent. In France, the CAC 40 declined 0.2 percent. Asian markets closed mostly higher. The Nikkei in Japan 225 rose 1.3 percent and the Kospi in South Korea gained 0.6 percent.
