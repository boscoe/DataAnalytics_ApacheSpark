AdvertisementSupported byBy The Associated PressStocks paused on Tuesday, closing moderately lower after five straight days of gains. DuPont and energy shares rose sharply, but the overall market was weighed down by health care stocks, especially biotechnology companies.Investors appear to be in standby mode, with the closely watched minutes from the Federal Reserve’s September meeting set to come out on Thursday and third-quarter company earnings just around the corner.The Dow Jones industrial average added 13.76 points, or 0.1 percent, to 16,790.19. The Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index lost 7.13 points, or 0.4 percent, to 1,979.92 and the Nasdaq lost 32.90 points, or 0.7 percent, to 4,748.36.The biggest gainer in the S.&P. 500 and Dow was the chemical giant DuPont, whose shares rose $3.93, or nearly 8 percent, to $55.21. On Monday, DuPont’s chief executive, Ellen Kullman, said she would retire on Oct. 16. DuPont’s profits have lagged in recent years, and the company has been a target of activist investors like Nelson Peltz.Biotechnology stocks were hit hard. The Nasdaq biotechnology index fell nearly 4 percent after the recently announced 12-nation Trans-Pacific Partnership trade deal provided only eight years of certain kinds of drug patent protection, less than the 12 years the industry had sought.Biotech stocks have plummeted in recent months because of investor concerns that the industry could face more scrutiny from Washington over its drug pricing practices. The index is down 24 percent from its peak in July.Barring some geopolitical crisis or shocking company news, the next major move for the market will most likely come Thursday, when investors get the minutes from the Fed’s policy meeting in September.Securities that allow investors to bet on which way the Fed will move interest rates now show the market expects the next rate increase will come in March 2016. The minutes, which break down the issues the Fed addressed, should provide clues on whether policy makers still feel confident about raising interest rates.“We need to see if they had signs that last week’s bad jobs report was coming, and decided to hold off on raising rates then, or if they are still set on moving this year,” said J. J. Kinahan of TD Ameritrade.In energy markets, oil rose after the Energy Department said American crude production declined by 120,000 barrels a day in September versus August. The agency also expects production to decline from an average of 9.2 million barrels a day this year to 8.9 million barrels in 2016.American benchmark crude jumped $2.27 to settle at $48.53 a barrel on the New York Mercantile Exchange. That helped send oil and gas companies sharply higher. ConocoPhillips, Chevron and ExxonMobil rose between 2 and 4 percent each.Brent crude, a benchmark for international oils used by many United States refineries, rose $2.67 to $51.92 a barrel in London.In other futures trading on the Nymex, wholesale gasoline rose 5.1 cents to $1.436 a gallon; heating oil rose 6.3 cents to $1.612 a gallon; and natural gas rose 2 cents to $2.47 per 1,000 cubic feet.United States government bond prices rose slightly. The yield on the 10-year Treasury note edged down to 2.04 percent. The dollar fell to 120.18 yen and the euro rose to $1.1278.In metals trading, the price of gold rose $8.70 to $1,146.80 an ounce, silver rose 28 cents to $15.98 an ounce and copper was little changed at $2.36 a pound.Advertisement
