By THE ASSOCIATED PRESSMARCH 26, 2015
A seesaw day for United States stocks ended with slight losses on Thursday, giving the market its fourth consecutive decline.Investors continue to monitor violence in the Middle East and the rapid ascent of the dollar, which is causing companies to reduce their profit forecasts for the year.After hitting nominal highs — not adjusted for inflation — this month, stocks have been steadily declining this week. Strategists and traders said the strong dollar, geopolitical tensions and a market that is already expensive have given investors little incentive to buy in recent days.“We have been due for a pullback,” said Brad Sorensen of the Schwab Center for Financial Research. Referring to the possibility that the Federal Reserve would raise interest rates this summer, he added, “The markets have been focused on the Fed and the impact of a stronger dollar.”AdvertisementThe Dow Jones industrial average lost 40.31 points, or 0.2 percent, to close at 17,678.23. The Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index lost 4.90 points, or 0.2 percent, to 2,056.15, and the Nasdaq composite index fell 13.16 points, or 0.3 percent, to 4,863.36.

The price of oil rose sharply as traders worried that mounting tensions in Yemen could disrupt the flow of crude from the Persian Gulf region. Saudi Arabia and other gulf states struck military installations in Yemen in an effort to oust Shiite rebels who had forced the country’s president to flee.United States crude rose $2.22, or 4.5 percent, to settle at $51.43 a barrel in New York. United States crude oil has risen 17 percent since hitting a low of $43.96 a barrel a week ago.Brent crude, a benchmark for international oils used by many American refineries, rose $2.71, or 4.8 percent, to close at $59.19 a barrel in London.“The conflict has the potential to act as a drag on oil supplies as most oil tankers from Arab producers must pass by the Yemen coastline in order to get through the Red Sea and Suez Canal,” said Craig Erlam, senior market analyst at Oanda.The rise in oil was not enough to lift battered energy stocks. The energy sector in the S.&P. 500 index ended the day down 0.2 percent.
