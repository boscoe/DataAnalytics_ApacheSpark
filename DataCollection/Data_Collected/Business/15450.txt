By THE ASSOCIATED PRESSAUG. 18, 2016
Stocks on Wall Street again ticked higher on Thursday as the continuing rebound in oil prices gave energy companies a lift. The gains were modest, however, as investors have been avoiding big moves.The dollar weakened further, and compared with the yen it is at its lowest in almost three years.Stocks wobbled in the early part of the day, but energy companies stood out as the price of oil reached its highest point since the beginning of July. The weakening dollar aided exporters, including technology and chemical companies. The market turned higher in the afternoon. Phone company stocks continued to slump, as did financial firms.Stocks have not moved much this week and have not made many big moves over the last month.The Dow Jones industrial average picked up 23.76 points, or 0.1 percent, to close at 18,597.70. The Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index added 4.80 points, or 0.2 percent, to close at 2,187.02. The Nasdaq composite rose 11.49 points, or 0.2 percent, to 5,240.15.Ryan Detrick, senior market strategist for LPL Financial, said Wall Street tends to be calm in August and trading volume is usually low. Lower trading volume means surprising events can cause big swings, but so far, this month has been the opposite of January and early February, when stocks tumbled and the markets were rattled.

“We had record volatility to start the year and these things do tend to revert,” he said. “It’s amazing how quickly things change.”AdvertisementBenchmark crude oil gained $1.43, or 3.1 percent, to $48.22 a barrel in New York. Brent crude, used to price international oils, rose $1.04, or 2.1 percent, to $50.89 a barrel in London. That gave energy companies a lift, and Marathon Oil rose 6.2 percent, while Devon Energy gained 3.3 percent.AdvertisementOil prices have rallied over the last two weeks but have essentially remained at $40 to $50 a barrel for the last four months. After oil traded as low as $26.21 a barrel in February, that relative stability has bolstered energy stocks.The dollar fell to 99.92 yen, its lowest point since October 2013. The euro rose to $1.1362 from $1.1279. The dollar was worth about 120 yen at the start of the year and has gradually weakened. A weaker dollar is good for American exporters like chemical, mining and technology companies.

Walmart climbed 1.9 percent after it reported strong results for the second quarter and raised its annual estimates. Walmart, which has lost sales to websites like Amazon as people make more purchases online, is also buying the online retailer Jet.com for about $3 billion as it fights for more online shoppers.Prison operating companies nose-dived after the Justice Department said it would end or sharply reduce contracts with them, saying privately run prisons have more problems with safety and security than prisons run by the government. Shares of the Corrections Corporation of America plunged $9.65, or 35.5 percent, to $17.57, and the Geo Group tumbled $12.78, or 39.6 percent, to $19.51.Bond prices inched higher, and the yield on the 10-year Treasury note fell to 1.53 percent from 1.55 percent.Gold added $8.50, rising to $1,351.20 an ounce. Silver gained 9 cents to close at $19.74 an ounce, and copper picked up 2 cents to close at $2.17 a pound.Germany’s DAX added 0.6 percent and the French CAC 40 index rose 0.4 percent. Britain’s FTSE 100 gained 0.1 percent. Hong Kong’s Hang Seng rose 1 percent and Tokyo’s Nikkei 225 slid 1.5 percent.
