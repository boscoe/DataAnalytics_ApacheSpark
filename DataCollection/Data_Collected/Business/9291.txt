By THE ASSOCIATED PRESSSEPT. 29, 2015
Fred A. Stickel, who turned The Oregonian into one of the country’s best regional newspapers during his 34 years as publisher, died on Sunday. He was 93.His death was confirmed by his family.The Oregonian won five Pulitzer Prizes during Mr. Stickel’s tenure, which ended with his retirement in 2009, at 87. He also hired the newspaper’s first African-American editor and its first female editor.Mr. Stickel was born on Nov. 18, 1921, in Weehawken, N.J.He joined the Marine Corps in 1942 and served in World War II. After the war, he sold newspaper advertising at The Jersey Journal, in Hoboken. He went to work for the Newhouse newspaper chain in 1951.The company moved Mr. Stickel and his family to Portland in 1967 so he could serve as general manager of The Oregonian. He became president five years later and publisher in 1975.AdvertisementIn 1982, Mr. Stickel closed the city’s afternoon paper, The Oregon Journal, and folded its staff into The Oregonian.He named Sandy Rowe as editor in 1993 and gave her permission to hire 50 editors and reporters, setting the stage for the paper’s run of Pulitzer-winning efforts.AdvertisementMr. Stickel is survived by two daughters, Daisy Medici and Bridget Otto; four sons, Fred Jr., Patrick, James and Geoffrey; and many grandchildren and great-grandchildren. His wife, the former Margaret Ann Dunne, died in 2008.A version of this article appears in print on September 30, 2015, on Page A19 of the New York edition with the headline: Fred A.
