AdvertisementSupported byBy The Associated PressTechnology and health care companies led a broad rally on Tuesday that pulled the Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index back into black for the year.The gains erased the market’s losses over the previous two days, when worries over falling prices for oil and other commodities had dragged down stocks. That trend ended on Tuesday when the price of United States crude oil rebounded with a 2.9 percent gain.Investors also drew encouragement from better-than-expected data on consumer confidence and housing.“Both of those set us up nicely on a low-volume day to be more positive than negative,” said Darrell Cronk, president of Wells Fargo Investment Institute.The Dow Jones industrial average rose 192.71 points, or 1.1 percent, to 17,720.98. The S.&P. 500 gained 21.86 points, or 1.1 percent, to 2,078.36. The Nasdaq composite added 66.95 points, or 1.3 percent, to 5,107.94.The S.&P. 500 index is now on course to end 2015 with a gain of about 1 percent. The Nasdaq is up nearly 8 percent for the year, while the Dow is down 0.6 percent.In Europe, Germany’s DAX rose 1.9 percent, while France’s CAC 40 gained 1.8 percent. Britain’s FTSE 100 rose 1 percent. Several markets in Asia showed small gains.The United States rally accelerated as investors got a look at the latest batch of the country’s economic data. The Conference Board said its consumer confidence index increased from the previous month, reflecting positive views on the economy and job market.Separately, a crucial gauge of home values indicated that United States home prices climbed 5.5 percent in October from a year earlier. Home values have risen at a pace of about 5 percent during much of 2015 as strong hiring bolstered the real estate market, which is still recovering from a bust that set off a recession eight years ago.Traders also welcomed a break in the decline in crude oil prices.Benchmark United States crude rose $1.06, or 2.9 percent, to settle at $37.87 a barrel on the New York Mercantile Exchange, recovering after a slump on Monday. Brent crude, which is used to price international oils, gained $1.17, or 3.2 percent, to settle at $37.79 a barrel in London.Consol Energy and Chesapeake Energy were among the companies to benefit from the rise in energy prices. Shares of Consol gained 37 cents, or 4.7 percent, to $8.24, while Chesapeake jumped 51 cents, or 12.5 percent, to $4.58.“Lately the markets have been taking their cue from the movement in commodity prices, and in particular, oil,” Mr. Cronk said.In other energy trading in New York, wholesale gasoline rose 4.3 cents, or 3.5 percent, to $1.276 a gallon; heating oil rose 3.9 cents, or 3.6 percent, to $1.23 a gallon; and natural gas jumped 14.4 cents, or 6.5 percent, to $2.372 per 1,000 cubic feet.Precious and industrial metals had higher closing prices. Gold rose $7.00 to $1,077.50 an ounce, silver rose 4 cents to $13.93 an ounce and copper gained 6 cents to $2.14 a pound.Bond prices fell. The yield on the 10-year Treasury note rose to 2.31 percent from 2.23 percent late on Monday. The dollar was slightly higher at 120.42 yen, up from 120.34 on Monday. The euro slipped to $1.0933 from $1.0972.Pep Boys surged 8.8 percent after the auto parts and services retailer received another offer from the activist investor Carl C. Icahn, valuing the company at about $1 billion. Shares in Pep Boys have been rising steadily over the last two months as a takeover bid from Japanese tire maker Bridgestone turned into a fight for control with Mr. Icahn.
