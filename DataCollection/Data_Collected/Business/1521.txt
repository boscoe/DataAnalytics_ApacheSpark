AdvertisementSupported byBy The Associated PressStocks gained ground early on Monday, coming off their best week this year, but faded in the afternoon and sustained small losses for the day.Technology stocks fell more than the rest of the market. The pharmaceutical company Pfizer and Allergan, the maker of Botox, slipped after announcing a deal to merge, which would create the world’s largest drug company. The video game retailer GameStop slipped after reporting weak results.The Dow Jones industrial average lost 31.13 points, or 0.2 percent, to close at 17,792.68. The Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index declined 2.58 points, or 0.1 percent, to 2,086.59. The Nasdaq composite index fell 2.44 points, or 0.1 percent, to 5,102.48. The S.&P. 500 index rose 3.3 percent last week, lifted by good news from retailers and technology stocks. That was its largest weekly gain since December. However, it was not quite enough to cancel out a big loss from the week before.Pfizer, the maker of Viagra and Lipitor, said it would buy Allergan for about $160 billion. Pfizer will gain new products that will help it counteract the expiration of patents protecting some drugs. Pfizer will also relocate to Ireland as part of the deal, reducing its tax bill.If the purchase goes through, it would be one of the largest corporate transactions in history. It is also the biggest deal this year so far — a year that has been filled with big mergers. Just two weeks ago, the maker of Budweiser, Anheuser-Busch InBev, agreed to buy rival beer maker SABMiller for about $105 billion.Pfizer’s stock decreased on Monday by 86 cents, or 2.7 percent, to $31.32, and Allergan’s stock fell $10.74, or 3.4 percent, to $301.72. The companies said last month that they were discussing a merger. Since then, Allergan’s shares are up about 6 percent, but Pfizer’s have lost 11 percent.Apple dragged down the technology sector as its stock fell $1.54, or 1.3 percent, to $117.76. GameStop’s stock decreased after it reported a smaller quarterly profit and less revenue than analysts had predicted. The company said that sales of new games and game systems were not as good as it expected. Its shares slid $1.64, or 4.2 percent, to $37.62. Shares of the video game maker Electronic Arts fell after GameStop’s announcement, losing $3.44, or 4.8 percent, to $68.98. Consumer discretionary stocks rose. Shares of Chipotle Mexican Grill rebounded $23.03, or 4.3 percent, to $559.22. On Friday, its stock dropped 12 percent and set an annual low because of reports that an E. coli outbreak linked to its restaurants had spread.Amazon’s stock rose $10.54, or 1.6 percent, to $678.99 days before Black Friday. Shares of Macy’s, which like many retailers has taken a beating on fears that the holiday shopping season will be weak, rose $1.46, or 3.8 percent, to $40.06. Tobias Levkovich, chief United States Equity Strategist for Citi Investment Research, said consumers would keep spending because energy costs and interest rates were low, businesses were hiring, and there were signs that wages are increasing.On Tuesday morning, the Conference Board will release its latest Consumer Confidence report, which will give investors more insight into how consumers are feeling and how much they will spend shopping.Shares of the British drug maker Mallinckrodt and the food producer Tyson both climbed after solid quarterly reports. Tyson climbed $4.45, or 10.2 percent, to $48.10. Mallinckrodt gained $5.10, or 8.4 percent, to $66.10. Mallinckrodt’s shares have plunged 47 percent since early August on concerns about new regulations of drug prices.Shares of Alcoa, an aluminum maker, rose after the hedge fund Elliott Management disclosed that it had bought a 6.4 percent stake in it. Alcoa gained 38 cents, or 4.4 percent, to $9.07. Benchmark United States crude oil fell 15 cents, or 0.4 percent, to settle at $41.75 a barrel in New York. Brent crude, which is used to price international oils, rose 17 cents, or 0.4 percent, to $44.83 a barrel in London.Heating oil edged up 0.3 cents to $1.37 a gallon. Wholesale gasoline gained 2.3 cents to $1.31 a gallon. Natural gas rose 6.5 cents, or 3 percent, to $2.21 per 1,000 cubic feet.Metals prices were mired in a slump that continued on Monday. Gold fell $9.50, or 0.9 percent, to $1,066.80 an ounce. Silver lost 6 cents, or 0.5 percent, to $14.03 an ounce. Copper slid 3.4 cents, or 1.7 percent, $2.02 per pound.United States government bond prices rose slightly. The yield on the 10-year Treasury note fell to 2.24 percent from 2.26 percent. The dollar rose to 122.85 yen from 122.84 yen. The euro declined slightly to $1.0625 from $1.0657 on Friday.Advertisement
