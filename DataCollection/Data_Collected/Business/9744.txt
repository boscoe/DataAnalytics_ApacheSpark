AdvertisementSupported byBy The Associated PressShaky results from consumer companies dragged the stock market lower on Tuesday as well-known names like the appliance maker Whirlpool and the athletic apparel maker Under Armour suffered their worst losses in years.Third-quarter earnings continued to dominate the market, and some of the biggest companies either reported disappointing results or lowered their expectations. Investors wondered if consumers would spend less on home improvement, clothing and other goods.But shares of companies including Procter & Gamble and Lockheed Martin soared after their reports. Looking for safer options, some investors bought bonds and utility company shares and also bid up the price of gold.Consumer spending is critical to the economy, and poor results for consumer-focused companies could be a sign of trouble. But Doug Roman, managing director of equities for PNC Capital Advisors, said it was too soon to know if shoppers were closing their wallets.“The market might be extrapolating bigger stories into broader themes, which might not be the case,” he said. Corporate earnings have been falling for more than a year, and despite the results on Tuesday, investors are growing hopeful that streak is ending.The Dow Jones industrial average shed 53.76 points, or 0.3 percent, to 18,169.27. The Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index lost 8.17 points, or 0.4 percent, to 2,143.16. The Nasdaq composite fell 26.43 points, or 0.5 percent, to 5,283.40.Sherwin-Williams, the maker of paints and coatings, posted a disappointing profit and cut its annual guidance because of slower sales growth combined with spending on new stores. Whirlpool’s results fell far short of analyst projections.Sherwin-Williams had its worst day in seven years as it lost $30.27, or 10.9 percent, to $247.61. Whirlpool, which owns Maytag and KitchenAid, sank $18.37, or 10.8 percent, to $152.09, its largest loss in five years.Under Armour reported its slowest sales growth in six years and said its future sales would not be as strong as it expected a year ago. Its stock tumbled $5.01, or 13.2 percent, to $32.89, its biggest drop in almost eight years. Its rival Nike also slipped.Procter & Gamble, which makes Tide detergent and Charmin toilet paper, had its best day in more than a year after it reported better results than investors expected. The consumer products giant has been selling some businesses to cut costs, and it posted stronger sales of personal care products like toothbrushes and deodorants. Its stock rose $2.87, or 3.4 percent, to $86.97.The aerospace and defense company Lockheed Martin surpassed investor forecasts and raised its projections for the year. Its stock gained $17.00, or 7.3 percent, to $249.26, its biggest leap in seven years.Earnings releases continued to stream in after the market closed. Apple reported lower quarterly sales and sold fewer iPhones, sending its stock down about 2 percent in late trading. The company did give a better-than-expected forecast for the holiday season.Also on Tuesday, Baker Hughes, an oil and gas drilling services company, disclosed a smaller loss than expected. Investors were also pleased that the company was preparing to cut more costs. Baker Hughes said it planned to eliminate $650 million in spending this year, up from the $500 million it had planned to cut. Its stock climbed $2.24, or 4.3 percent, to $54.39.The drugmaker Merck raised its forecasts after it reported a bigger profit thanks to greater sales of vaccines and cancer treatments. The company has also been trying to keep its spending in check. The stock rose $1.21, or 2 percent, to $61.95.The 3M Company, which makes Post-it Notes, industrial coatings and ceramics, forecast weaker sales growth and a smaller profit for the year. The stock declined $5.04, or 2.9 percent, to $166.23.Nielsen, the media and marketing information company, took its biggest loss ever after it reported shaky results and cut its guidance. Nielsen stock tumbled $9.28, or 16.9 percent, to $45.65.Benchmark crude lost 56 cents, or 1.1 percent, to $49.96 per barrel in New York. Brent crude, the international standard, fell 67 cents, or 1.3 percent, to $50.79 a barrel in London.Bond prices edged higher. The yield on the 10-year Treasury note fell to 1.76 percent from 1.77 percent. Investors bought shares of utility companies and sought out precious metals amid uncertainty. The price of gold futures for October delivery rose $9.90 to $1,271.90 an ounce on the New York Mercantile Exchange.The dollar slipped to 104.19 yen from 104.21 yen.
