AdvertisementSupported byBy The Associated PressSolid earnings reports on Thursday kept the market advancing in record territory.Stocks rose from the start of trading after JPMorgan Chase released results that were better than analysts expected. Companies are expected to report lower earnings in the April-June period, but the few big ones that have released numbers so far have beaten low expectations, encouraging investors.Nine of the 10 industry sectors of the Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index showed gains. Banks rose the most, 0.9 percent.Investors pulled money out of conservative assets like gold and Treasury bonds, sending yields on the bonds sharply higher. They also sold stocks of utility companies, considered a haven because of their safe and steady dividends.The Dow Jones industrial average rose 134.29 points, or 0.7 percent, to 18,506.41. The S.&P. 500 gained 11.32 points, or 0.5 percent, to 2,163.75.The Nasdaq composite increased 28.33 points, or 0.6 percent, to 5,034.06.The Dow and S.&P. 500 remain at record highs; the Nasdaq is barely positive for the year.The United States gains followed rallies in Germany, France and Japan. Stock indexes in each of those countries rose more than 1 percent.British stocks initially rose, then gave up the gains after the Bank of England surprised investors by holding off on cutting interest rates despite the hit to the British economy from last month’s vote to leave the European Union. The British pound soared on the news.American stocks have been on a rocky ride since the start of the year, falling at the beginning of the year on fears of a slowdown in China, and again after Britain’s vote to leave the European Union. But investor spirits were lifted by a strong United States jobs report last Friday, hopes that Japan’s ruling party will flood its market with even more money and signs of political stability in Britain.Brad McMillan, chief investment officer at the Commonwealth Financial Network, said the recent scares may have flushed out sellers. “Anyone inclined to sell did so” earlier, he said.Among companies making big moves on Thursday, the Japanese messaging app Line surged 27 percent on its first day of trading, a gain of $8.74, to $41.58. Line has more users than Facebook or Twitter in Japan.Delta Air Lines rose $1.42, or 3.6 percent, to $40.98 after also reporting second-quarter profits that beat expectations. Lower fuel prices have helped airlines post big profits.JPMorgan Chase rose 98 cents, or 1.6 percent, to $64.12. Results topped profit forecasts; investors were also encouraged by strong trading revenue for the quarter. Citigroup and Wells Fargo report results on Friday.The DAX in Germany was up 1.4 percent and the CAC 40 in France rose 1.2 percent. The British FTSE 100 slipped 0.2 percent.In Japan, the Nikkei 225 closed nearly 1 percent higher. The Hang Seng in Hong Kong rose 1.1 percent.United States government bond prices fell, pushing the yield on the 10-year Treasury note to 1.54 percent from 1.47 percent.The British pound rose to $1.3327 from $1.3136 a day earlier. The euro rose to $1.1111 from $1.1092. The dollar rose to 105.33 yen from 104.42 yen.Benchmark United States crude rose 93 cents to close at $45.68 a barrel in New York. Brent crude, a standard for international oil prices, rose $1.11 to close at $47.37 a barrel in London.Wholesale gasoline rose 4 cents, to $1.41 a gallon. Heating oil rose 3 cents, to $1.41 a gallon.
