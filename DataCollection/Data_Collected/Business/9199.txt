By THE ASSOCIATED PRESSSEPT. 24, 2015
More evidence that global economic growth is slowing weighed down the United States stock market for a third straight day on Thursday.The market fell sharply at its open — stocks dropped close to their lowest levels of the month — before rebounding during afternoon trading to close with only slight losses.Shares of Caterpillar, a bellwether for industrial companies, slumped after it cut its sales outlook for this year and announced that it would eliminate as many as 10,000 jobs between now and 2018 to cut costs.

Also, the government reported that orders for long-lasting United States manufactured goods dropped in August. The Commerce Department said that an important indicator of business investment spending was especially weak.Advertisement“We’re looking for that good news, and we’re not getting any,” said John Toohey, vice president for equity investments at USAA, a privately held bank and insurance company.AdvertisementThe Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index fell 6.52 points, or 0.3 percent, to close at 1,932.24. The Dow Jones industrial average lost 78.57 points, or 0.5 percent, to 16,201.32. The Nasdaq fell 18.27 points, or 0.4 percent, to 4,734.48.The market has been depressed for the last month as investors worry that the slowdown overseas, particularly in China, will hurt American companies. A decision this month by the Federal Reserve to hold its benchmark interest rate close to zero also made investors uneasy.

The S.&P. 500 has fallen 9.2 percent since setting a benchmark high of 2,130.82 in May and is on track for its second straight month of losses.On Thursday, Caterpillar, the maker of mining and construction equipment, was the biggest decliner in the S.&P. 500. Its stock fell after it cut its 2015 revenue estimate by $1 billion to about $48 billion. Caterpillar also said sales would fall another 5 percent next year. Its stock dropped $4.41, or 6.3 percent, to $65.79.Caterpillar is just one of the mining companies suffering from a global slump in commodity prices.European markets also fell. The DAX in Germany dropped 1.9 percent. The FTSE 100 in Britain declined 1.2 percent. The CAC 40 in France lost 1.9 percent.In commodities trading, benchmark United States crude rose 43 cents, to $44.91 a barrel, on the New York Mercantile Exchange.
