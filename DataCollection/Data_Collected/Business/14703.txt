AdvertisementSupported byBy The Associated PressStocks slumped on Tuesday as investors grew fearful over the health of the British financial system.Investors were jolted after three British financial firms stopped trading in their commercial property funds because large numbers of investors were trying to liquidate their holdings.Looking for safety, investors flocked to Treasury notes and pushed the yields on long-term government bonds to record lows. Energy companies took the biggest losses as oil prices tumbled.Stocks mostly fell, although investors bought shares of companies seen as safe plays, like household goods makers and utilities. Bond yields plunged, and the 10-year and 30-year Treasury yields reached record-low levels as demand for Treasuries rose and prices increased.The yield on the 10-year note dropped to 1.38 percent, a record low according to Tradeweb, down from 1.45 percent late Friday. It went as low as 1.36 percent during the day, according to Tradeweb. The yield on the 30-year note fell to 2.16 percent, also a record. It was 2.24 percent Friday.It was an abrupt end to a big four-day rally for stocks, and a reminder that the effects of Britain’s vote to leave the European Union has left markets deeply unsettled.“We’ve seen a tremendous rally pretty much every night in longer-term bonds” since the vote, said Tom di Galoma, managing director at Seaport Global Holdings. “There’s just so many unanswered questions both from the legal standpoint, a diplomatic standpoint, an economic standpoint.”The Standard & Poor’s 500-stock index slid 14.40 points, or 0.7 percent, to 2,088.55. The Nasdaq composite lost 39.67 points, or 0.8 percent, to 4,822.90.The Dow Jones industrial average fell 108.75 points, or 0.6 percent, to 17,840.62. American markets were closed on Monday for the Independence Day holiday.On Tuesday, the trouble began when Aviva Investors, Standard Life and M&G Investments stopped trading in their commercial property funds. The firms said they were protecting other investors who wished to remain in their respective funds. The Bank of England said it eased bank rules to allow them to lend up to 150 billion pounds ($200 billion) to households and businesses.The pound fell to $1.3032 from $1.3259 on Tuesday, its weakest in 31 years.Traditionally steady investments did well. Gold rose $19.70, or 1.5 percent, to $1,358.70 an ounce. Silver gained 32 cents, or 1.6 percent, to $19.91 an ounce. Copper lost 3 cents, to $2.18 a pound. Gold is at its highest price in more than two years and silver is the highest it has been since August 2014.Benchmark United crude oil sank $2.39, or 4.9 percent, to close at $46.60 a barrel in New York. Brent crude, used to price international oils, fell $2.14, or 4.3 percent, to close at $47.96 a barrel in London. That pulled energy companies lower.In other energy trading, wholesale gasoline fell 8 cents, or 5.6 percent, to $1.43 a gallon. Heating oil lost 7 cents, or 4.4 percent, to $1.45 a gallon. Natural gas dropped 22 cents, or 7.5 percent, to $2.76 per 1,000 cubic feet.Outside the United States stock indexes were mostly lower. The CAC 40 in France fell 1.7 percent, and the German DAX lost 1.8 percent. However, the British FTSE 100 picked up 0.4 percent. Japan’s benchmark, the Nikkei 225, slipped 0.7 percent to finish and the South Korean Kospi fell 0.3 percent. In Hong Kong, the Hang Seng dipped 1.4 percent.The dollar fell to 101.49 yen from 102.58 yen. The euro slid to $1.1075 from $1.1125.Advertisement
