By THE ASSOCIATED PRESSSEPT. 29, 2016
Stocks on Wall Street skidded Thursday as drug companies and banks absorbed large losses. Drugmakers faced scrutiny over price increases, while banks fell as investors worried about the stability of Deutsche Bank and other financial institutions.Stocks were slightly lower in morning trading, and they fell hard at midday on renewed concerns about Deutsche Bank, Germany’s largest bank. EpiPen’s maker, Mylan, fell after lawmakers called for an investigation of the company. And the price of oil continued to rise, which sent oil drilling and equipment companies higher. Stocks gave up most of their gains from the last two days.The Dow Jones industrial average lost 195.79 points, or 1.1 percent, to 18,143.45. The Standard & Poor’s 500 index sank 20.24 points, or 0.9 percent, to 2,151.13. The Nasdaq composite dropped 49.39 points, or 0.9 percent, to 5,269.15.Quincy Krosby, market strategist for Prudential Financial, said investors did not trust Deutsche Bank’s statements about its financial health and were worried about what would happen to the bank and to the broader financial system if Deutsche Bank ran low on capital.

The company gave up $1.75, or 4.4 percent, to $38.47. The stock is down 21 percent since mid-August as the company has come under criticism for repeatedly raising EpiPen’s price over the last decade.Other drug companies traded lower as investors worried that the government would take action to rein in drug price increases. Merck fell $1.39, or 2.2 percent, to $61.91. Amgen fell $4.26, or 2.5 percent, to $165.45.Oil prices continued to rise after a 5 percent surge the day before. Energy prices had jumped after the nations of OPEC, which collectively produce more than a third of the world’s oil, agreed to a small cut in production.

United States benchmark crude picked up 78 cents, or 1.7 percent, to $47.83 a barrel in New York. Brent crude, the international benchmark, added 55 cents, or 1 percent, to $49.24 a barrel in London. Companies that drill for oil, and those that provide rigs and other equipment to drillers, gained as investors figured higher prices for oil would spur more demand. But companies that refine oil fell on the prospect of paying more for crude.CBS and Viacom rose after Sumner Redstone’s National Amusements, which controls both companies, said it wanted them to combine again. CBS and Viacom merged in 1999 and split up in 2006. Viacom rose $1.21, or 3.3 percent, to $37.77, and CBS rose 42 cents to $54.57.Bond prices moved higher.
