The culture of jazz nourishes itself on the past, but not because it has nothing better to do. It’s because there are challenges and contradictions there; the past can burn us or correct us or make us see ourselves more clearly. The surprise is always that the past always has more to show us, and in ways we hadn’t anticipated. Those ideas kept surfacing in a year-end Popcast conversation Jon Pareles, Nate Chinen and I had recently about the movements of jazz in 2014: fascinating new-old records (current work meditating or somehow commenting on history and tradition, like David Virelles’s “Mboko,” George Cables’s “Icons and Influences,” Miguel Zenón’s “Identities Are Changeable,” Jason Moran’s “All Rise: A Joyful Elegy for Fats Waller,” Mostly Other People Do the Killing’s “Blue”); or old-new records (previously unreleased live recordings by John Coltrane, by Jim Hall and Charlie Haden, and by the Keith Jarrett-Charlie Haden-Paul Motian trio).
