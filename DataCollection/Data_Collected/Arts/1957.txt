By MEGHAN RICE and ANNE MANCUSOJAN. 1, 2015
Museums and SitesAmerican Museum of Natural History: ‘Lonesome George’ (through Sunday) His death in 2012 brought to an end the species of this Pinta Island tortoise of the Galápagos Islands, named Lonesome George for his largely singular existence. But thanks to naturalists here and in Ecuador and taxidermy specialists, he can be viewed as he appeared in life, with neck and limbs extended; following this exhibition, he will be returned to Ecuador. Daily from 10 a.m. to 5:45 p.m.; Central Park West and 79th Street, 212-769-5200, amnh.org.American Museum of Natural History: ‘Pterosaurs: Flight in the Age of Dinosaurs’ (through Sunday) The earliest known vertebrates to fly, pterosaurs large and small roamed the skies more than 60 million years ago. This exhibition examines the lives of these creatures with fossils, casts and life-size models, as well as videos and interactive simulations. Daily from 10 a.m. to 5:45 p.m.; Central Park West and 79th Street, 212-769-5200, amnh.org.Brooklyn Historical Society: Nell Painter and Sarah Lewis in Conversation (Tuesday) This free discussion is being presented in conjunction with the exhibition “She Said, She Said: Art and Inspiration in the Work of Nell Painter and Lucille Fornasieri Gold.” The author Sarah Lewis will talk with Ms. Painter, a historian turned artist, about her inspirations and decision to delve into the visual arts. 128 Pierrepont Street, near Clinton Street, Brooklyn Heights, 718-222-4111, brooklynhistory.org.Grolier Club: ‘One Hundred Books Famous in Children’s Literature’ (through Feb. 7) Early editions of children’s literary classics printed from 1600 to 2000 — including “Robinson Crusoe,” “Alice’s Adventures in Wonderland” and “Tom Sawyer” — are on display along with more than 50 artifacts. Monday through Saturday from 10 a.m. to 5 p.m., 47 East 60th Street, Manhattan, 212-838-6690, grolierclub.org; free.AdvertisementIntrepid Sea, Air & Space Museum: ‘Masters of Disguise: The World of Camouflage’ (through Feb. 24) The art of camouflage in nature and its appropriation by hunters and the armed forces are explored in this exhibition, which uses digital images, artifacts and interactive elements to showcase how color, shade and shapes can be used to deceive the eye. Daily, from 10 a.m. to 5 p.m., Pier 86, 46th Street and 12th Avenue, Clinton, 877-957-7447, intrepidmuseum.org.AdvertisementMuseum of Chinese in America: ‘Who Was Shuck W. Chin?’ (Sunday) Heather Lee, whose “A Life Cooking for Others: The Work and Migration Experiences of a Chinese Restaurant Worker in New York City, 1920-1946” was part of the anthology “Eating Asian America: A Food Studies Reader,” weaves personal effects, letters and household items together into a portrait of a restaurant worker in postwar Chinatown. At 2 p.m., 215 Centre Street, between Howard and Grand Streets, Lower Manhattan, 212-619-4785, mocanyc.org.National Museum of Math: ‘Robot Swarm’ This exhibition features two dozen small, glowing robots that have been programmed to communicate with one another and react to people’s movements, either following them or zooming away. In addition to the fun, the show will feature material that explains the simple mathematical rules behind swarm technology. Daily from 10 a.m. to 5 p.m., 11 East 26th Street, Manhattan, 212-542-0566, momath.org.New-York Historical Society: ‘Chinese American: Exclusion/Inclusion’ (through April 19) In his review for The New York Times, Edward Rothstein called it a “vigorous and imaginative new exhibition,” that includes a look at “how Chinese-American identity came to be, how immigration and discrimination were followed by recrimination and conflict, and how, finally, a people’s trials give way to celebration.” Tuesday through Thursday and Saturday from 10 a.m. to 6 p.m., Friday till 8 p.m.; Sunday from 11 a.m. to 5 p.m.; closed on Monday; 170 Central Park West, at 77th Street, 212-873-3400, nyhistory.org.New York Public Library for the Performing Arts: ‘Emily Dickinson: Musician and Poet’ (through March 6) Emily Dickinson’s poems and letters as well as the library’s collections of her work provide the backdrop for this exhibition focusing on the musical side of her writing. Dickinson’s musical identity, which included piano performances, the collection of sheet music and concerts outings, will be detailed. Monday and Thursday from noon to 8 p.m.; Tuesday, Wednesday, Friday and Saturday from noon to 6 p.m.; 111 Amsterdam Avenue, at 65th Street, 917-275-6975, nypl.org/lpa; free.New York Transit Museum Annex: Holiday Train Show (through Feb. 22) This free annual holiday treat for model train enthusiasts, featuring vintage Lionel trains, is on display in Grand Central Terminal. Weekdays from 8 a.m. to 8 p.m.; Saturdays and Sundays from 10 a.m. to 6 p.m.; New York Transit Museum Gallery Annex and Store at Grand Central Terminal, near the Station Master’s Office; mta.info/mta/museum.Schomburg Center for Research in Black Culture: First Fridays: Black Male Edition (Friday) In conjunction with the center’s “Question Bridge: Black Males” exhibition, this free evening of entertainment and discussion will focus on the experiences of black men. Among the highlights are a screening of Spike Lee’s “Get on the Bus” and music from DJ Masai, who will put his musical spin on the night’s theme. At 6 p.m., 515 Lenox Avenue, at 135th Street, Harlem, 212-491-2206, schomburgcenter.org.Smithsonian’s National Museum of the American Indian: Photography and Jewelry Display (through Feb. 15) More than 80 black-and-white photographs and vintage postcards from the 1920s to ’60s, featuring the work of the American Indian photographer Horace Poolaw, who died in 1984, are the focus of the exhibition “For a Love of His People,” on view through Feb. 15. His camera is also part of the display, which chronicles American Indian life on the Southern Plains of Oklahoma. Another exhibition, “Glittering World: Navajo Jewelry of the Yazzie Family,” a collection of 300 pieces of jewelry, runs through Jan. 10. Daily from 10 a.m. to 5 p.m.; until 8 p.m. on Thursdays; 1 Bowling Green, Lower Manhattan, 212-514-3700, nmai.si.edu; free.AdvertisementGardensNew York Botanical Garden: Holiday Train Show and ‘Bar Car Night’ (through Jan. 19) Landmarks made of natural materials, including new models of Grand Central Terminal and the garden’s conservatory, are included in this year’s display. And there are, of course, the model trains that whiz by the luxuriant foliage. On Friday and Saturday from 7 to 10 p.m., visitors 21 and older can view the display while sipping cocktails and listening to jazz during the “Bar Car Night” series, which runs selected days through Jan. 10. Train show hours are Tuesdays through Fridays and Sundays from 10 a.m. to 6 p.m.; Saturdays from 10 a.m. to 7 p.m.; Bronx River Parkway (Exit 7W) and Fordham Road, the Bronx, 718-817-8700, nybg.org.EventsCelebration of American Circus (Sunday) The Big Apple Circus, Les 7 Doigts de la Main and Philippe Petit are among the artists and organizations instrumental in revolutionizing the circus arts. This weekend they will be honored at the first Celebration of American Circus, presented by Circus Now. The event, directed by West Hyler, will be both a ceremony and performance, and will be followed by a reception. At 8 p.m., Damrosch Park, Lincoln Center, circusnow.org/celebrate.An Evening with Annie Baker (Thursday) This playwright is celebrating the publications of two of her plays, “The Flick,” winner of the 2014 Pulitzer Prize, and her translation of Chekhov’s “Uncle Vanya.” The night will also include performances from both plays and a discussion between Ms. Baker and Adam Greenfield, director of new play development at Playwrights Horizons. At 7 p.m., McNally Jackson, 52 Prince Street, between Lafayette and Mulberry Streets, SoHo, 212-274-1160, mcnallyjackson.com.Holiday Duo Recital  (Sunday) The Wagner Society of New York presents this recital with the soprano Jennifer Benke, the tenor Ric Furman and the pianist Tom Bagwell performing works by R. Strauss, Medtner, Barber, Giordano, Sibelius and Wagner. At 3 p.m., Marjorie S. From 7 to 8:30 p.m., National Jazz Museum in Harlem, 104 East 126th Street, 212-348-8300, jazzmuseuminharlem.org.92nd Street Y: A Reading and a Discussion (Tuesday and Wednesday) Ilyasah Shabazz, Malcolm X’s daughter, and Kekla Magoon celebrate the release of “X: A Novel,” a book based on the actual events of Malcolm X’s life. Sunny Hostin, of CNN, will moderate the talk, which starts at 7:30 p.m. on Tuesday. The next evening, at 7:30 p.m., two of the country’s security experts, John Miller, the city Police Department’s deputy commissioner of intelligence and counterterrorism, and Jane Holl Lute, a former deputy secretary of homeland security, will discuss tips for safe travel. 1395 Lexington Avenue, 212-415-5500, 92y.org.‘A Path Appears’ Screening (Wednesday) Nicholas Kristof, an Op-Ed columnist for The New York Times, and Sheryl WuDunn, his wife and a former business editor at The Times, were joined in this documentary by celebrity activists (including Mia Farrow, Jennifer Garner, Regina Hall and Eva Longoria) as they traveled across the world to uncover the everyday difficulties of women and girls. The film, by Maro Chermayeff, was developed in conjunction with Mr. Kristof and Ms. WuDunn’s book, “A Path Appears: Transforming Lives, Creating Opportunity.” The free screening is from 6:30 to 8:30 p.m., and reservations are suggested. Anne Frank Center USA, 44 Park Place, at Church Street, Lower Manhattan, 212-431-7993, annefrank.com.Three Kings Day Parade (Tuesday) In the Bible, the three kings followed a bright star to Bethlehem, where they presented gifts to the baby Jesus. On Tuesday at 11 a.m., they will lead a procession in East Harlem as part of El Museo del Barrio’s 38th annual Three Kings Day Parade, with camels, thousands of schoolchildren, There will also be giant handmade puppets, music and dancing at this free event, which begins at 106th Street and Lexington Avenue and ends at 115th Street and Park Avenue. At the end of the parade route, traditional Puerto Rican music will be played and food will be available from noon to 1 p.m. The celebration — sponsored by the City Council speaker, Melissa Mark-Viverito; La Marqueta Retoña; Acacia Network; and the New York City Economic Development Corporation — will continue until 2 p.m. at El Museo del Barrio, with a musical performance by Pablo Mayor and friends. Ms. Farran’s has invited, among others, James Wolcott of Vanity Fair and the New York Magazine critic Matt Zoller Seitz to take part. At 7 p.m., 828 Broadway, at 12th Street, 212-473-1452, strandbooks.com.Jason Sokol and Brent Staples (Monday) Mr. Sokol, author of “All Eyes Are Upon Us: Race and Politics From Boston to Brooklyn,” will discuss his book and a range issues related to race with Brent Staples, an editorial writer for The New York Times.
